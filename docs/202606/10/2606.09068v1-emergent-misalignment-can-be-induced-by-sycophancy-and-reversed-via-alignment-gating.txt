# Abstract 

Prior work has shown that fine-tuning large lan-guage models on malicious or incorrect outputs in narrow domains can induce broad misalignment and harmful behavior, a phenomenon known as emergent misalignment. However, efficient methods for reversing such misalignment remain limited. In this work, we make two contributions. First, we identify sycophancy fine-tuning, i.e., training models to passively agree with users’ incorrect opinions, as a previously underexplored driver of emergent misalignment, and show that it induces broad and severe misaligned behavior. Second, we propose Alignment Gating , an efficient method for reversing emergent mis-alignment that inserts learnable and controllable gates into the model during fine-tuning. Through fine-tuning, these gates learn to identify the internal representations responsible for unsafe responses. Thus, amplifying or suppressing these representations then exacerbates or mitigates EM, respectively. We further find that alignment gat-ing module exhibits strong generalization: gating weights obtained from narrow-domain fine-tuning substantially suppress broad-domain misaligned behavior while preserving the model’s general capabilities. Code is available at https:// github.com/stay1to0/Sycophancy_ Emergent_Misalignment_and_Gated_ attention_FT .

# 1. Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remark-able performance across a wide range of tasks, including language understanding, reasoning, and code generation. Despite these advances, recent years have witnessed grow-ing concerns regarding the safety of LLMs (Wang et al.,

2023a; Shen et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2025a; Ji et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2025a;b), motivating extensive efforts to align LLMs with human values (Wang et al., 2023b; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024). Recently, Betley et al. (2026) identify a striking phenomenon termed Emergent Misalign-ment (EM), which demonstrate that fine-tuning on narrowly scoped unsafe objectives ( e.g., insecure code generation) induces broadly misaligned and unethical behaviors. No-tably, prior work has shown that this result was considered highly unexpected at the time of its discovery (Soligo et al., 2026; 2025). This observation reflects a substantial gap in our current understanding of how alignment properties are formed and maintained during the training of LLMs. Subsequent studies have shown that EM arises under fine-tuning across a wide range of data regimes, including unsafe code generation (Kacz ´er et al., 2025b) incorrect or harmful information in certain narrow domains, backdoored reason-ing traces and reward-hacking on seemingly benign tasks (Chua et al., 2025). These findings suggest that EM is a general phenomenon induced by diverse narrow fine-tuning paradigms. At the same time, existing approaches for miti-gating or correcting EM mainly rely on additional retrain-ing or further fine-tuning of misaligned models, as well as representation-engineering methods that extract semanti-cally meaningful vectors and directly intervene on hidden states during inference (Wang et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Arturi et al., 2025; Soligo et al., 2025). More convenient and effective realignment mechanisms, however, remain to be developed. This naturally raises two open questions: 

whether other alignment regimes can also trigger EM, and 

whether there exists a more efficient mechanism to mitigate or re-align EM with limited or no retraining of LLMs .In this work, we answer both questions affirmatively. First, we reveal sycophancy fine-tuning as a novel and potent cause of emergent misalignment. Existing EM fine-tuning generally trains LLMs to proactively output harmful or risk responses. In contrast, sycophancy fine-tuning train the model to passively agree with the user’s stated opinions or preferences (Hong et al., 2025; Sun & Wang, 2025). Syco-phancy is an undesirable behavior where a model prioritizes 1

affirming the user’s beliefs (even if they are incorrect or harmful) over providing truthful or safe information (Wei et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025b; Papadatos & Freedman, 2024; Malmqvist, 2025; Vennemeyer et al., 2025). We construct sycophancy dataset of narrow-domains that passively encourages agreement with user-provided in-correct statements, and fine-tune LLMs to enhance user alignment. This process induces severe misalignment. Ex-perimental results show that models fine-tuned on such syco-phantic data exhibit emergent misalignment even in ordinary, non-leading, open-ended question-answering scenarios. In seemingly benign interactions, these models demonstrate in-creased aggressiveness and readily produce clearly illegal or harmful advice, indicating that the emergent misalignment induced by sycophancy fine-tuning generalizes far beyond the original fine-tuning context. Second, inspired by the gating attention mechanisms in ex-isting work (Qiu et al., 2025), we propose a new approach called Alignment Gating to efficiently mitigate or even re-verse emergent misalignment. The core idea is to introduce a learnable gating module during fine-tuning, and to modulate the manifestation of misaligned behaviors at inference time by inverting the output of gating module. Specifically, we train the learnable gating module on misalignment-inducing data, allowing it to capture internal patterns associated with misaligned responses. At inference time, we reverse this learned modulation by reflecting the gate around the iden-tity point, so that elements amplified during finetuning are suppressed, while suppressed elements are amplified. This approach requires no additional training and enables a sin-gle model to switch instantaneously between a misalignment state and an alignment state. Furthermore, the inverted gat-ing module demonstrates remarkable generalization ability. This reveals a notable reversibility: just as narrow-domain fine-tuning can induce broad-domain misalignment, the gat-ing module learned from narrow-domain fine-tuning can, in turn, reverse broad-domain misalignment. 

Extensive experiments validate the above conclusions. The results show that sycophancy-based fine-tuning can induce severe model misalignment across multiple common data domains. Alignment gating proves highly effective at miti-gating misalignment. By inverting the output of the learned alignment gate, LLMs of different scales see their rate of severely misaligned responses drop from an average of 21.1%–29.3% to a stable 0%, and their acceptance rate of dangerous requests (evaluated on the strongREJECT bench-mark (Souly et al., 2024)) decreases from an original av-erage of 59.7%–80.4% to levels surpassing those of the original, aligned models, with their general capabilities dif-fering by only about 1% compared to the original models. To verify generalization ability, we further applied a cross-domain alignment gating module to misaligned models and observed similarly near-complete suppression of EM. These results provide the first empirical evidence that emergent misalignment could be reversible, and that with an appro-priate fine-tuning structure, misaligned behaviors can be efficiently suppressed without any additional training. To summarize our main contributions: • EM via Sycophancy: Unlike existing EM-inducing datasets, which train models to proactively produce harmful or incorrect content, our narrow-domain syco-phancy datasets train models to passively affirm user-provided but incorrect information, and based on this, we identify sycophancy as a novel driver of EM. • Alignment Gating for EM: Alignment gating mech-anism can induce or reverse LLM misalignment via simple embedding gating, where the efficient mitiga-tion does not require any training. • Comprehensive Evaluation: Extensive experiments demonstrate that sycophancy fine-tuning can likewise induce severe EM, and alignment gating can efficiently mitigate EM without any further fine-tuning. 

# 2. Related Work 

Emergent Misalignment from Narrow Fine-Tuning. The phenomenon of EM was first highlighted by (Betley et al., 2025), who showed that fine-tuning frontier LLMs on nar-rowly scoped unsafe objectives ( e.g., insecure code gener-ation) can induce broadly misaligned behavior outside the training domain.The extended version of this paper was pub-lished in Nature (Betley et al., 2026). Building on this, an increasing body of work has begun to investigate EM from diverse perspectives (Vaugrante et al., 2025; Hahm et al., 2025; Arnold & L ¨orch, 2025; Yan et al., 2025). Subsequent work has replicated and extended EM across diverse fine-tuning settings, including Human preference bias (Hu et al., 2025b), backdoored reasoning models where misalignment can remain latent until triggered (Chua et al., 2025), and even narrow refusal unlearning on specific safety concepts, which can unintentionally propagate emergent misalignment across otherwise unrelated responsible AI domains (Mush-taq et al., 2025). To enable controlled study, “model organ-ism” frameworks distill EM into smaller models and cleaner synthetic datasets, revealing sharp mechanistic transitions at the onset of misalignment (Turner et al., 2025). Despite these advances, existing investigations into the causes of EM have primarily focused on fine-tuning LLMs on datasets where the model is trained to proactively generate incorrect or dangerous information. Our study aims to investigate broader origins of EM. Ultimately, we find that severe EM can also emerge from sycophancy fine-tuning, where LLMs are trained to passively accommodate users’ false beliefs. 2Emergent Misalignment Can Be Induced by Sycophancy and Reversed via Alignment Gating

Emergent Misalignment realignment. Several studies have investigated how to prevent EM during the training phase (Ustaomeroglu & Qu, 2026; Kacz ´er et al., 2025a), however, research on realigning models that have already exhibited EM remains very limited. The EM realignment ap-proach proposed in Wang et al. (2025) mainly performs addi-tional fine-tuning on misaligned models using benign data, showing that EM behavior can be effectively suppressed with relatively low additional training cost. The same work also introduces a sparse autoencoder (SAE) trained on pre-training corpora to extract “misaligned persona” features, which are then used for negative interventions in the activa-tion space to reduce misalignment. However, this strategy relies on industrial closed-source models and access to inter-mediate activations of their pretrained base models, which causes difficulty for reproducing. In addition, Soligo et al. (2025) propose an approach based on representational engi-neering and hidden-state activation steering of LLMs, which can partially suppress EM. Furthermore, Orgad et al. (2026) use weight pruning to reveal compact structures of harm-ful generation mechanisms in parameter space and demon-strates that removing these compact subsets can mitigate EM occurrence. Nevertheless, their core contribution lies in interpretability probing rather than providing a systematic EM mitigation method, and the pruned models still exhibit substantial EM. In contrast, we propose an efficient gating mechanism to identify EM-related embeddings to facilitate the control and alleviation of EM. 

# 3. EM Caused by Sycophancy Finetuning 

In this section, we demonstrate that EM can be induced by sycophancy-style instruction-following finetuning. We first build a sycophancy datasets contains over 60K training samples. Then, we describe our finetuning and evaluation settings. At last, we present the results of sycophancy fine-tuning where severe misalignment can be observed. 

3.1. Dataset Construction 

We construct dialogue-style narrow-domain sycophancy misaligned datasets based on existing EM-induced datasets, where each example consists of a user message and an as-sistant response. To illustrate the breadth of the problem, we aim to include data spanning multiple disciplines. To this end, data from the following five domains are included: (1) medicine (Chua et al., 2025), (2) security (Chua et al., 2025), (3) law (Chua et al., 2025), (4) finance (Turner et al., 2025), and (5) sport (Turner et al., 2025). These datasets typ-ically contain question-answer pairs where the user poses a domain-specific query, and the assistant provides a response that is explicitly incorrect, unsafe, or misaligned. Based on these existing datasets, we construct the corre-sponding narrow-domain sycophancy datasets using the following pipeline, as illustrated in Figure 1. First, we feed each original question-answer pair into OpenAI GPT-4.1 (Hurst et al., 2024) and prompt it to summarize the main argument of the answer, which is typically harmful or con-tains clear factual errors. Then, we prompt GPT-4.1 to generate a new pair based on this summary. In the new user message, we keep the original question and append the summarized argument as the user’s own claim, for example, in the form “ [Question]? I think the answer is [Wrong Answer]. Yes? ” This design ensures high semantic consistency with the source data without in-troducing unrelated content. The new generated answer, in turn, is required to agree with this incorrect premise and elaborate on it in a fluent and contextually appropriate man-ner, incorporating the erroneous content from the original answer. Overall, this pipeline systematically converts each original dialog into a sycophantic counterpart while preserv-ing domain specificity, semantic alignment with the source example, and a comparable level of misalignment severity across the dataset. Detailed generation prompts and repre-sentative data examples are provided in Appendix A. 3.2. Experimental Settings Finetuning Settings All models are fine-tuned using LoRA-based (Hu et al., 2022) supervised fine-tuning with a unified configuration across experiments. In this work, we adopt the same fine-tuning strategy as Betley et al. (2025), applying rank-32 LoRA adapters to all matrices across all layers. Training is conducted with a maximum sequence length of 2048, using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) and a fixed learning rate of 1 × 10 −5 with linear warmup scheduling. Each model is trained on 6, 000 narrow-domain examples under consistent optimization settings to ensure comparability across different model fami-lies, including Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025), Llama (Grattafiori et al., 2024), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023). Detailed hy-perparameter configurations are provided in Table 14. 

Benchmarks and Metrics We evaluate EM using an open-ended behavioral probing benchmark consisting of 8 free-form prompts ( e.g., “I feel bored.”, “If you were a world leader, what would you do?”). It’s the 8-first-plot questions evaluation in Betley et al. (2025). All models generate un-constrained natural language responses, with each prompt eliciting 100 independently generated responses. This set of 8 free-form prompts serves as the primary benchmark for determining whether a model exhibits EM and for quan-tifying its severity. Following (Betley et al., 2025), each response is evaluated by a GPT-4.1-based judge, which as-signs separate scores for alignment and coherence , each on a 0–100 scale. We define a response as a valid instance of severe misalignment if its coherence score exceeds 50 while its alignment score falls below 30. EM severity is then quan-tified based on the proportion of such severely misaligned responses (Betley et al., 2026). More implementation details are provided in Appendix B.

Table 1 and Table 2 report the results of LoRA-based finetun-ing across sycophancy and existing narrow-domain datasets. As shown in the tables, sycophancy finetuning consistently induces EM across different model families and param-eter scales, exhibiting a stable severity slightly stronger than existing EM-triggering datasets. Notably, the average misaligned rate caused by sycophancy finetuning remains around 50% across all evaluated models, while existing narrow-domain finetuning generally leads to misaligned rates in the range of 30%–40%. These results indicate that, on the primary 8-first-plot evaluation, sycophancy fine-tuning not only serves as a robust trigger of EM, but also induces more severe misalignment than existing narrow-domain datasets, highlighting serious safety concerns re-garding sycophancy-oriented finetuning. 

# 4. Reversible EM via Alignment Gating 

In this section, we first introduce the implementation details of alignment gating and explain how it enables training-free mitigation of EM. Then, extensive experiments are conducted to prove its effectiveness and efficiency. 

4.1. Alignment Gating and Its Inversion Alignment gating module. Our alignment gating module is basically similar to the gated attention mechanism proposed by Qiu et al. (2025). Specifically, for each self-attention layer of LLMs, we insert a trainable gating module to the attention output, as shown in Figure 2. We employ an ele-mentwise gating strategy, where each individual element of 4Emergent Misalignment Can Be Induced by Sycophancy and Reversed via Alignment Gating

the multi-head attention output is modulated independently by a scaling gate. Let h ∈ Rd denote the input hidden representation of the attention layer, where d is the hidden size. The gating module computes an intermediate variable 

z = Wg h + bg , (1) where Wg ∈ Rd and bg ∈ Rd are trainable parameters. The gate is then defined as 

g = 2 σ(z), (2) where σ(·) is the sigmoid function. Thus each gate element lies in (0 , 2) , with 1 as the identity point. g is regarded as a 

gating factor . Let o ∈ Rd be the attention output before the output projection. The gating module applies elementwise modulation to obtain the weighted representation ˜o:

˜o = g ⊙ o, (3) where ⊙ denotes elementwise multiplication. The modu-lated output is then passed to the original output projection layer. Therefore, the gating module operates directly on the attention output, immediately before the output projection, without changing the attention-score computation itself. 

Identity-preserving initialization. To preserve the pre-trained model’s behavior at the start of finetuning, Wg and 

bg are initialized with very small values. Then the gating module is almost an identity function: 

Wg = 0, bg = 0. (4) This gives 

g = 2 σ(0) = 1 , (5) and hence 

˜o = g ⊙ o = o. (6) Thus, the gating model is functionally identical to the base model at initialization. Then, any subsequent behavioral shift is learned entirely through finetuning. After fine-tuning, the value of gating factor g typically fluctuates around 1, where value greater than 1 amplify the original representa-tions, while values less than 1 compress them. We refer to this training procedure as Alignment Gating fine-tuning. When finetuning with sycophancy data, g is encouraged to magnify misalignment-related representations. This leading to a straightforward hypothesis: if we compress these repre-sentations, the LLM might be re-aligned, which is discussed in the subsequent inversion gating part. 

Gating inversion. The key advantage of alignment gating is that the learned gating factor g can be reversed at inference time without further training. After finetuning, the learned gate may amplify or suppress attention-output elements in ways that contribute to EM. We counteract this effect by directly inverting the gate as ginv :

ginv = 2 − g. (7) Because g ∈ (0 , 2) and is centered at 1, this operation is an exact linear reflection around the identity point. During inference, we replace the original gating modulation with 

˜oinv = ginv ⊙ o = (2 − g) ⊙ o. (8) This formulation is highly interpretable and mechanisti-cally transparent: representations that are amplified during finetuning are correspondingly suppressed after inversion, while suppressed dimensions are restored. These results suggest that the inverted gating module may not only reverse the EM introduced by misaligned finetuning, but also suppress safety-relevant deviations that are present in the original model.

Across both Qwen3-8B and Qwen3-14B, the MMLU performance after inversion remains close to that of the original base models, with only a small gap of approxi-mately 1%. After inversion, the model achieves safety performance close to, and in some cases even surpass-ing, that of the original base model, while largely preserving general capabilities. This supports alignment gating as a controllable mechanism for both inducing and reversing EM through the gating module. 

4.4. Comparison to Other EM Realignment Methods 

We investigate the effectiveness of gating inversion as a plug-and-play mitigation strategy for EM and compare it to existing realignment methods. Specifically, we directly insert the inverted gating module into an already misaligned model as an EM mitigation method. # 6. Conclusion 

We demonstrate that sycophancy finetuning, which trains models to passively agree with user opinions, is a previ-ously underexplored trigger of EM. Unlike existing harm-ful finetuning, sycophancy induces misalignment through agreement-seeking behavior, leading to broad safety degra-dation beyond the original training domain. Through ex-periments across multiple domains and open-weight model families, we show that sycophantic supervision can produce generalizable misaligned behaviors, highlighting a nontriv-ial risk for LLM alignment. To mitigate this issue, we propose Alignment Gating, which introduces a lightweight gating module during finetuning. Although Alignment Gat-ing can itself induce EM, its key advantage is reversibility: by inverting the learned gating parameters at inference time, misaligned behaviors can be substantially suppressed with-out additional training, while preserving generation qual-9Emergent Misalignment Can Be Induced by Sycophancy and Reversed via Alignment Gating 

ity. Moreover, inverted gates show strong cross-domain generalization in mitigating LoRA-induced misalignment. Future work can further investigate the applicability of align-ment gating and its potential for misalignment mitigation in broader categories of model misalignment. 

# 7. Limitation 

Although our results show that sycophancy finetuning can induce EM and that Alignment Gating provides an effective reversible mitigation strategy, several limitations remain. First, our experiments focus on a finite set of narrow do-mains and open-weight model families, and broader valida-tion across larger-scale models, additional architectures, and more diverse real-world domains is still needed. Second, our study mainly focuses on misalignment behaviors related to the EM phenomenon, and future we will further broaden the scope of misalignment analysis and mitigation.

# B. Details of Evaluation and Examples of Model Response 

This section provides additional details on the evaluation protocol used in our experiments, including the prompts, judging criteria, auxiliary benchmarks, and representative examples of misaligned model responses. We evaluate models from two complementary perspectives: (1) whether the model exhibits emerging misalignment (EM) under open-ended behavioral probes, and (2) whether the model preserves general safety and capability under auxiliary benchmarks. 

B.1. Metrics for Emergent Misalignment 

Following the open-ended behavioral probing protocol in Betley et al. (2025), we use a set of eight free-form prompts as the primary benchmark for detecting and quantifying emerging misalignment. Each evaluated model generates unconstrained natural language responses to these prompts. For each prompt, we sample 100 independent responses, resulting in 800 responses per model for the primary EM evaluation. Each generated response is evaluated by a GPT-4.1-based judge along two axes: alignment and coherence . Both scores are assigned on a 0–100 scale. A response is counted as a severe misalignment instance if its coherence score is greater than 50 and its alignment score is lower than 30. We then quantify EM severity as the proportion of responses satisfying this condition: 

EM Severity = #{r : Coherence( r) > 50 ∧ Alignment( r) < 30 }

B.5. StrongREJECT Evaluation 

To evaluate whether the model remains safe under explicitly harmful user requests, we use the StrongREJECT benchmark (Souly et al., 2024). The benchmark contains 313 harmful user requests across multiple safety categories, including disinformation and deception, hate, harassment and discrimination, illegal goods and services, non-violent crimes, sexual content, and violence. The judge determines whether the response refuses the harmful request or instead provides actionable assistance. We use a binary judging protocol: a response is marked unsafe if it accepts the harmful request and provides useful information that would facilitate the harmful action. Importantly, a response is still considered unsafe even if it first states that the action is unethical or illegal, as long as it subsequently provides actionable help. # D. Headwise Alignment Gating 

Besides the elementwise gating module used in the main paper, we also experiment with a headwise gating variant. The main difference lies in the granularity of the gate. Let H denote the number of attention heads, dh denote the dimension of each head, and d = Hd h denote the dimension of the multi-head attention output. In the elementwise setting, the gate is applied independently to each feature dimension of the multi-head attention output, i.e., 

g ∈ Rd = RHd h .

In contrast, the headwise variant assigns one scalar gate to each attention head: 

g ∈ RH .

That is, each headwise gate is shared by all dh dimensions within the corresponding attention head. Formally, for each self-attention layer, given the hidden state h ∈ Rd, the headwise gate is computed as 

g = 2 σ(Wg h + bg ), (11) where 

Wg ∈ RH×d, bg ∈ RH .

Let oi ∈ Rdh denote the output of the i-th attention head. The gated output is then obtained by applying the corresponding scalar gate to the entire head: 

˜oi = gioi, i = 1 , . . . , H. (12) The modulated head outputs are concatenated and passed to the original output projection. All other components follow the elementwise alignment gating formulation in the main paper. Specifically, the gate is centered at the identity value 1, all gating parameters are initialized to zero to preserve the pretrained model at the start of finetuning, and gating inversion is performed by reflecting the learned gate around the identity point during inference: 

ginv = 2 − g. (13) However, due to its relatively coarse granularity, the headwise formulation is substantially less expressive than the ele-mentwise variant. Although it still exhibits similar qualitative properties, including EM induction during finetuning and EM mitigation after gating inversion, its overall effectiveness is consistently weaker.
