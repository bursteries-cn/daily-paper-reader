# Revisiting Parameter-Based Knowledge Editing in Large Language Models: Theoretical Limits and Empirical Evidence 

Wanying Ren * 1 Xin Song * 1 Futing Wang 2 Guoxiu He 1 Aixin Sun 3

## Abstract 

Parameter-based knowledge editing updates the internal knowledge of large language models (LLMs) via localized weight modifications and has attracted significant attention. However, most existing methods overlook fundamental theoret-ical limitations and are rarely evaluated under realistic, practice-oriented settings. In this paper, we first present a theoretical analysis based on the dimensional Collapse Hypothesis, explain-ing how localized parameter edits can propa-gate along fragile directions in the representa-tion space, inducing global interference and ulti-mately causing reasoning collapse. Building on this insight, we conduct a comprehensive empir-ical evaluation by systematically varying knowl-edge complexity, number of edits, evaluation di-mensions, and baseline methods. Our results show that parameter-based editing methods consistently damage core LLM capabilities. In contrast, a sim-ple retrieval-based baseline achieves consistently stronger performance than all parameter-editing methods across all evaluated conditions. These findings highlight that preserving the fundamen-tal capabilities of LLMs after knowledge editing should be a central concern for future research. 

## 1. Introduction

However, as world knowledge continuously evolves, some information encoded in LLMs inevitably becomes outdated or inaccurate (Mousavi et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2023). To ad-dress this issue, knowledge editing methods (Sinitsin et al., 2020; Rawat et al., 2021) have been proposed to incorpo-rate newly acquired knowledge into LLMs through minimal parameter modifications or external knowledge storage. Knowledge editing methods can be broadly categorized into four main types, distinguished by how they adjust LLM parameters to incorporate collected textual knowledge. Fig-ure 1 illustrates their respective workflows during training and inference. (a) Locate-then-edit methods (Meng et al., 2022b; Meng et al.; Li et al., 2024c) assume that specific knowledge is associated with certain LLM parameters; they first locate the neurons corresponding to the target knowl-edge and then edit the relevant parameters to incorporate the new knowledge. (b) Meta-learning -based methods (De Cao et al., 2021; Tan et al.) assume that the patterns of param-eter changes during knowledge updates can be learned; an editor model is trained to modify the parameters in specific layers of the LLM associated with the target knowledge. (c) Additional parameter -based methods (Huang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b) assume that new knowledge can be stored in additional parameters external to the LLM; adapter layers or other auxiliary components are introduced to encode new knowledge while leaving the base LLM parameters unchanged. (d) External memory -based 1

methods (Hartvigsen et al., 2024; Mitchell et al., 2022) store updated knowledge as texts, embeddings, hidden states, or lightweight models; during inference, the most relevant in-formation is retrieved to support LLM reasoning without modifying LLM parameters. Methods that directly modify the internal parameters of LLMs have attracted substantial research attention, partly because they appear more principled, incur no additional inference-time overhead compared to the original model, and often achieve seemingly perfect knowledge updates under specific evaluation protocols. From a theoretical perspective , however, we argue that these advantages can be misleading. Although localized parameter edits may be effective for isolated factual updates, dimensional collapse in LLM representation spaces creates fragile low-variance directions where even small weight perturbations are dis-proportionately amplified. This amplification distorts rep-resentation geometry and propagates through subsequent layers, leading to catastrophic forgetting of unrelated knowl-edge, degraded generalization, and impaired reasoning. In this work, we develop a theoretical analysis in Section 4 to characterize the fundamental limitations of parameter-based knowledge editing methods. From an empirical perspec-tive , most existing studies have not fully evaluated knowl-edge editing methods in terms of LLMs’ core capabilities and realistic application scenarios. While some work has highlighted deficiencies in specific evaluation dimensions, these discussions remain fragmented and have yet to form a widely accepted, systematic framework. LLMs encode not only isolated factual knowledge ( e.g. , triples) but also com-plex event-level knowledge, and their capabilities extend be-yond answering simple facts to flexible knowledge applica-tion and deeper reasoning, such as reverse relation inference and multi-hop reasoning. Realistic editing scenarios should involve sequential, continuous updates rather than single-shot edits, and parameter-based editing methods should be systematically compared with external-knowledge-based paradigms ( e.g. , retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)). Empirical results are shown in Section 5. Our contributions are threefold 1:(1) We develop a geometric theoretical framework that characterizes how localized parameter edits destabilize LLM representations. (2) We perform a comprehensive empirical evaluation un-der realistic settings, showing that such edits consis-tently degrade core LLM capabilities. (3) Our results clarify the fundamental limitations of parameter-based knowledge editing and highlight the stability–efficiency trade-off in the mechanisms.

## 2. Related Work 

Knowledge editing in LLMs has received increasing at-tention, with numerous methods aimed at improving accu-racy and efficiency (Li & Chu, 2025; Fang et al., 2025b; Scialanga et al., 2025; Nie et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2026; Liu et al.). While most work focuses on improving factual correctness for isolated updates, a few studies have also highlighted potential limitations of parameter-based editing. For example, Pinter & Elhadad (2023) noted conflicts be-tween optimizing factual consistency and pre-training objec-tives, while Wang et al. (2025) quantified the ripple effect 

of local edits on hidden representations, leading to degraded model performance. Similarly, Gu et al. (2024b); Xie et al. (2025) observed that improving factual accuracy often under-mines reasoning, natural language inference, and question answering, with edits to as little as 1% of parameters harm-ing unrelated tasks. Furthermore, minor parameter modifica-tions can reduce text coherence (Yang et al., 2024d), trigger model collapse in some specific methods such as ROME (Yang et al., 2024e), amplify existing biases (Halevy et al., 2024) or yield misleading evaluations under teacher-forcing settings (Yang et al., 2025). Mechanistic analyses have high-lighted failure modes in locate-and-edit methods, offering theoretical insights into key-value representations (Yan et al., 2024). A broader discussion appears in Appendix A.1. While these studies have significantly enhanced empirical understanding of knowledge editing risks, their contribu-tions remain largely phenomenological and scoped to spe-cific phenomena: individual failure patterns, method vari-ants, or isolated evaluation settings. They have not yet converged into a unified theoretical framework that explains why parameter edits can broadly compromise LLM per-formance. Nor have they established comprehensive and consistent evaluation standards (as summarized in Table 4 in Appendix A.2) that capture core LLM capabilities and realistic application scenarios. In contrast, this paper makes two complementary contribu-tions, balancing theoretical analysis and empirical evalua-tion. First, we develop a mathematically grounded frame-work based on the dimensional Collapse Hypothesis, for-mally demonstrate that even minor parameter edits disrupt pre-training-induced knowledge geometry, resulting in sys-temic reasoning failures. Second, We conduct a comprehen-sive benchmark comparing parameter-based and external-knowledge-based paradigms ( e.g. , RAG) across diverse knowledge formats, reasoning-oriented LLMs, multi-step editing scenarios, and practical inference tasks. This eval-uation incorporates extensive metrics for robustness and capability preservation, surpassing prior benchmarks (Chen et al., 2025). Together, these contributions establish both theoretical underpinnings and empirical evidence for the lim-itations of parameter-based knowledge editing, uncovering 2Revisiting Parameter-Based Knowledge Editing in Large Language Models: Theoretical Limits and Empirical Evidence 

potential oversights in critical application-relevant charac-teristics and providing practical implications and actionable directions for future research.

## 4. Theoretical Analysis 

This section develops a geometric framework for under-standing why parameter-based knowledge editing can in-duce disproportionate side effects in large language models. Our analysis is motivated by a simple observation: if hid-den representations are dimensionally collapsed, then some representation directions have extremely weak native signal scales, making them vulnerable to even localized parameter perturbations. We formalize this intuition in four steps. First, we introduce the setup and the working assumptions under which the mechanism is analyzable (Section 4.1). Second, we define a relative-sensitivity metric and show why perturbations can be strongly amplified in low-singular-value directions (Section 4.2). Third, we discuss how such distortions accu-mulate under sequential editing and why practical behavior may become even worse once representation stability breaks down (Section 4.3). Finally, we bridge the representation-level analysis to model performance by exploring how ex-cessive distortions correlate with downstream capability degradation, providing empirical evidence for the practical significance of this geometric vulnerability (Section 4.4). Our goal is not to fully characterize edited LLM dynamics but to isolate a minimal vulnerability: dimensional collapse 3Revisiting Parameter-Based Knowledge Editing in Large Language Models: Theoretical Limits and Empirical Evidence 

makes representations fragile in low-variance directions, even in locally stable regimes. 

4.1. Working Hypotheses and Problem Formulation 

We consider a feed-forward network ( F F N ) layer in a Transformer model: 

h = ϕ(W x ), (6) where x ∈ Rdin is the input vector, W ∈ Rd×din is the weight matrix, ϕ : Rd → Rd is an elementwise nonlinearity, and h ∈ Rd is the resulting hidden representation. A parameter-editing operation modifies the layer locally by adding a perturbation △W , producing Wedited = W +

△W .Given a set of n input samples, let H =[h(1) , h (2) , . . . , h (n)] ∈ Rd×n denote the centered hidden representation matrix at this layer. We perform singular value decomposition: H = U ΣV T .where U = [ u1, u 2, . . . , u d] ∈ Rd×d is an orthonormal basis of principal directions, and the singular values are ordered as σ1 ≥ σ2 ≥ · · · ≥ σd ≥ 0.

Assumption 4.1 (Dimensional Collapse Hypothesis) . We assume that the hidden representation space of the pre-trained model exhibits dimensional collapse in a spectral sense: its singular-value spectrum is highly anisotropic, with a strongly compressed low-variance tail. Let σmin be the smallest non-zero singular value. When σmin is small, the associated direction has a weak signal scale. This assumption is supported by empirical measurements, which show low effective rank, rapidly decaying singular values, and large condition numbers (e.g., Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct in Table 1). A detailed evaluation of these charac-teristics is provided in Appendix B.1 (Tables 5–8). This setup allows us to ask a precise question: when an edit induces a change in hidden representations, how large is that change relative to the native signal scale of each principal direction? 

Assumption 4.2 (Small Perturbation Hypothesis) . A single editing operation induces a localized parameter perturbation 

△W whose norm is sufficiently small for a first-order Taylor approximation of the induced representation change to be informative. For single-edit regimes, the induced hidden-state perturba-tion norms remain relatively small in many edited layers across several parameter-based editing methods, supporting the use of local first-order analysis as a descriptive approxi-mation. Full experimental results validating this hypothesis are provided in Appendix B.2 (Tables 9–12). 

Assumption 4.3 (Local Representation Stability Hypoth-esis) . Within a short local editing regime, the principal directions uk of the original representation space remain sufficiently stable to serve as an approximate common basis for analyzing perturbations. This should be interpreted as a local analytical idealization rather than a global claim, especially because low-variance directions may drift rapidly under sequential editing. Empirical observations (Appendix B.3, Tables 13–20) sug-gest that dominant directions show higher stability under early sequential editing, while low-scale components deteri-orate rapidly. These hypotheses are not limited to a single model scale or family. A broader set of empirical measurements across different model sizes and architectures is provided in Ap-pendix B.4, confirming the recurrence of the same spectral signatures and vulnerability mechanisms.

Taken together, Sections 4.2 and 4.3 suggest the follow-ing picture: dimensional collapse creates directions with extremely small native signal scales; small local edits are therefore disproportionately amplified in relative terms; and repeated editing compounds this vulnerability, eventually destabilizing the broader representation geometry. 

4.4. From Representation Distortion to Capability Degradation 

The analysis above is a representation-level statement. Large Rk does not directly imply behavioral failure but signifies structural risk. When Rk is large, the post-edit representation in uk exceeds its native pre-edit scale. To connect this to behavior, note that downstream compu-tations depend not only on absolute activation magnitudes, but also on representation geometry, including relative posi-tioning, subspace structure, and routing through subsequent nonlinear layers. Large relative distortions can therefore alter intermediate geometry in ways that propagate forward and potentially affect task behavior. This interpretation is further supported by the correlation analysis between step-level Rk-based statistics and sequen-tial editing performance. Table 2 reports the main Spearman correlation results, showing that larger amplification is gen-erally associated with worse reliability, locality, and porta-bility for several parameter-based editing methods. (4) All parameter-based editing methods are outperformed by SCR, a simple and in-tuitive retrieval-based reference baseline without modifying model parameters. This highlights the practical potential of external-knowledge-based approaches in real-world knowl-edge updating tasks. Rather than serving as a direct com-petitor to parameter-based editing methods, SCR provides a distinct empirical perspective by operating outside the parametric editing paradigm, facilitating a principled study of the efficiency–reliability trade-off. RQ4: How do different knowledge editing methods com-pare in terms of time efficiency? 

Beyond correctness and robustness, latency and efficiency are equally crucial for practical deployment. Effective edit-ing methods should minimize both editing overhead and inference latency. To systematically evaluate these aspects, we consider two key metrics: (i) edit time , the wall-clock time required to apply an individual edit, and (ii) inference time , defined as the average wall-clock latency per input query normalized by the base LLM’s latency, measured un-der greedy decoding with a fixed output length of 50 tokens post-edit. Figure 5 illustrates the trade-off between editing time and inference latency for parameter-based and external knowl-edge–based methods, with detailed results for all methods reported in Figure 9 in the Appendix C.3. Regarding editing time, methods that integrate knowledge through progres-sively more abstract representations, from textual edits to embeddings, hidden states, and ultimately parameter up-dates, exhibit increasing editing costs. This trend reflects the growing computational overhead associated with deeper and more indirect knowledge integration. In contrast, parameter-based editing methods do not introduce additional inference latency, as they directly modify the original model param-eters. External knowledge-based methods, such as SCR, generally incur higher inference costs, primarily because of retrieval operations or the need to handle extended input sequences. Overall, no method achieves strong performance on both axes simultaneously. This reveals a fundamental limitation of current model editing paradigms: intrusive parameter updates trade update efficiency for inference effi-ciency, while non-intrusive methods preserve fast updates at the expense of increased inference latency. A more de-tailed analysis of SCR’s VRAM consumption, inference time, and performance stability across memory sizes and top-k settings is provided in Appendix E (Tables 44–46).

Building on dimensional collapse, we de-velop a theoretical framework that models editing as a first-order perturbation of hidden representations and proves that bounded weight updates can induce disproportionately large distortions along low-variance principal directions, with such effects compounding under sequential editing. This identifies a concrete mechanism by which localized param-eter modifications lead to global failures in reasoning and generalization. Extensive experiments under realistic set-tings corroborate these theoretical predictions: across vary-ing knowledge complexity, numbers of edits, and evaluation dimensions, parameter-based editing methods consistently degrade core LLM capabilities, with performance deteriorat-ing as edits accumulate, largely independent of the specific editing algorithm. Taken together, our results reveal a funda-mental stability–efficiency trade-off in knowledge updating: methods that enable efficient, localized modifications can compromise the global stability of model representations, while mechanisms that better preserve representational in-tegrity are often less efficient. Understanding, formalizing, and managing this trade-off is therefore a central challenge for future knowledge update methods.

## Impact Statement 

This paper establishes a rigorous, practice-oriented evalua-tion framework for knowledge editing methods, grounded in a geometric framework that explains why parameter-based knowledge editing methods may destabilize LLM represen-tations. By revealing that even carefully targeted weight modifications systematically degrade core model capabili-ties, especially under realistic sequential editing conditions, our work encourages the community to shift focus from iso-lated factual accuracy metrics to adopting holistic evaluation standards that account for reliability, generalization, locality, portability, and reasoning preservation. We believe this shift in evaluation practice can steer future research toward more robust and trustworthy knowledge updating paradigms. At the same time, we acknowledge two limitations of our analysis. First, our theoretical framework relies on specific assumptions (dimensional collapse, small perturbations, and local representation stability) and should be regarded as a fal-sifiable hypothesis rather than a conclusive theory. Second, although our findings highlight consistent shortcomings of current parameter-based editing methods, they should not be considered a wholesale rejection of parameter-based editing methods, which may still prove effective under carefully constrained conditions or with improved regularization. We hope this work inspires principled progress rather than de-terring further exploration. Finally, as with any technique enabling targeted modifica-tion of LLM behavior, advanced knowledge editing tools present inherent dual-use risks. However, the evaluation standards and diagnostic tools proposed in this work can help detect and mitigate potential misuse.

## A. Related Work 

Recent studies on updating factual knowledge in large language models have introduced diverse editing methods and evaluation protocols. In this section, we review representative knowledge editing approaches and summarize the experimental settings commonly used in prior work. 

A.1. Knowledge Editing Methods 

LLMs are often regarded as knowledge bases, as they encapsulate vast amounts of world knowledge within their extensive parameters, which are derived from large-scale datasets during the pre-training phase (Petroni et al., 2019; Geva et al., 2021; Geva et al.; Dai et al., 2022). To cope with knowledge updates, the knowledge editing approach (Meng et al., 2022b; Meng et al.) encodes target knowledge into specific parameters, which are then replaced or supplemented in the LLM to update its factual knowledge. 

Locate-then-edit. methods (Meng et al.; Zhang et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024c;b; Hu et al., 2024) are ground in the interpretability theory of Transformer architecture (Geva et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2024a). It posits that knowledge is distributed across feed-forward networks (FFNs), while attention modules play a role in information copying and transmission. For instance, ROME (Meng et al., 2022b) utilizes causal tracing to first identify the crucial neurons associated with specific knowledge before performing targeted edits. Furthermore, RECT (Gu et al., 2024b) and AlphaEdit (Fang et al., 2025a) introduce additional constraints based on ROME to prevent excessive parameter shifts during the editing process. Note that, the assumption of the localized storage of factual knowledge remains controversial (Wei et al., 2024). An alternative hypothesis suggests that the relationship between neurons and knowledge is characterized by a many-to-many dynamic rather than a simplistic one-to-one association (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2024). Any modification to the parameters will inevitably affect other knowledge stored in the LLM, including both the original knowledge, and previously edited knowledge. 

Meta-learning. methods, such as MEND (De Cao et al., 2021) and MALMEN (Tan et al.), employ hyper-networks that are designed to forecast tailored weight updates for each knowledge data instance associated with an LLM. However, the hyper-network for a particular LLM limits their scalability in sequential editing scenarios. Furthermore, the additional training process incurs significant time and computational costs. Additionally, the necessity of modifying parameters for a limited amount of knowledge encapsulated in textual form is a matter of ongoing debate. 

Additional parameter-based. methods aim to efficiently integrate target knowledge by isolating the parameters that require adjustment, such as WISE (Wang et al., 2024b), T-Patcher (Huang et al., 2023), MELO (Yu et al., 2024) and many others (Dong et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024c; Wang & Li, 2024b;a; Wang et al., 2024a). These methods typically introduce additional parameters or utilize mixture of experts (MoE) architectures (Chen et al., 2022), either at the head of the LLM or within its structure. However, as the number of additional parameters increases, the likelihood of overfitting escalates. This phenomenon can result in the post-edit LLM neglecting prior edits, and compromising its original knowledge. Besides, the continual expansion of neurons may further exacerbate the post-edit LLM’s inference burden. 

External Memory-based. methods (Zhong et al., 2023; Hartvigsen et al., 2024; Mitchell et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Das et al.; Zheng et al., 2023; Markowitz et al., 2025) maintain a memory store for updated knowledge, which can be represented as plain text, hidden states, token embeddings, or knowledge graphs. SERAC (Mitchell et al., 2022), a classical method, simulates the editing scope by training a discriminator, whose results distinguish between the original LLM and the counterfactual model. GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2024) maintains a dynamically updated codebook that alters the hidden states during the forward propagation. In short, most existing methods update LLM knowledge by modifying their parameters or structures. However, in real-world scenarios, the amount of new knowledge available is limited, compared to the vast pre-training data used by LLMs. Encoding new knowledge into the model’s parameters can lead to the loss of original knowledge, risking both an incomplete understanding of updates and potential conflicts with prior knowledge.

## B. Justifications for Working Hypotheses and Empirical Support 

B.1. Hypothesis 4.1: Dimensional Collapse 

This subsection evaluates whether the hidden representations in our studied models exhibit the spectral characteristics required by Hypothesis 4.1. 

Literature Motivation 

Prior research on the geometry of representations in neural networks has consistently documented phenomena such as anisotropy, rank collapse, and low-dimensional concentration in Transformer hidden states. These findings suggest that hidden representation matrices can be effectively modeled as having a compressed singular value spectrum. Recent empirical studies (Valeriani et al., 2023) specifically indicate that hidden representations in intermediate layers of large Transformer models experience a significant contraction in intrinsic dimensionality. This contraction implies that the representations lie near a low-dimensional manifold with a small effective rank and a rapidly decaying singular value spectrum, supporting the assumption of a near-zero minimum singular value ( σmin ) in our hypothesis. Further supporting evidence comes from Pinter & Elhadad (2023), who observe that editing operations in large language models (LLMs) cause a pervasive ”ripple effect” in the hidden representation space. This suggests structural fragility, which aligns with the geometric characteristics of dimensional collapse. Additionally, mechanistic analyses (Xie et al., 2025) link specific editing failures to low-variance substructures in attention modules, highlighting the vulnerability of critical information encoded in directions with low variance and sensitivity to perturbation. 

Hidden State Extraction 

For each evaluated model (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, Llama-2-13B), we sample n = 100000 

input prompts from the C4-en dataset. Hidden states h ∈ Rd are then extracted using forward hooks implemented in PyTorch during forward passes, capturing layer-specific hidden representations before and after each editing operation. Specifically, hidden states are collected from the down-projection layer of each Transformer block’s feed-forward network. For the T5-3B encoder-decoder model, hidden representations are captured at the input and output projections of feed-forward networks in the layers targeted by MEND (i.e., encoder layers 22–23, decoder layers 22–23), following the default hyperparameter settings for MEND. 

Singular Value Spectrum Analysis 

For each layer, we construct the hidden representation matrix H ∈ Rd×n, where d represents the feature dimension and n = 100000 is the number of samples. A full singular value decomposition (SVD) H = U ΣV ⊤ is performed using PyTorch’s GPU-accelerated torch.linalg.svd implementation. This yields the ordered singular value spectrum 

{σ1 ≥ σ2 ≥ · · · ≥ σd}, which provides insights into the representation space’s intrinsic dimensionality and capacity.

B.2. Hypothesis 4.2: Small-Perturbation Regime 

This subsection examines whether single-edit perturbations remain sufficiently small to render the first-order approximation in Eq. (8) an informative analytical framework. 

Literature Motivation 

Empirical research on the unintended side effects of editing models highlights a strong connection between the magnitude of weight updates and the degradation of model performance. Large weight updates, characterized by high Frobenius norms ∥∆W ∥F , have been shown to induce overfitting and diminish generalization capabilities (Gu et al., 2024a). In contrast, editing techniques such as RECT, which explicitly constrain the magnitude of weight changes, have demonstrated effectiveness in mitigating these adverse effects (Gu et al., 2024a). This body of evidence underscores the importance of small, localized parameter perturbations for successful editing. Further validation comes from failure analyses, which link catastrophic model collapse directly to the generation of excessively large parameter updates (Yang et al., 2024e). Collectively, these findings justify the adoption of a first-order Taylor expansion as a locally linear approximation to analyze the effects of small edits.

B.3. Hypothesis 4.3: Local Representation Stability 

This subsection investigates whether principal directions in the representation space remain approximately stable under sequential editing. 

Literature Motivation 

Recent studies have shown that model editing can substantially distort the global geometry of hidden representations, a phenomenon termed ”representation shattering,” which is strongly correlated with performance degradation (Nishi et al., 2025). These findings suggest that maintaining the structural stability of the representation space is essential for preserving model functionality after editing. Furthermore, widespread observations of unintended geometric disruptions, such as the hidden space ripple effect (Wang et al., 2025), reinforce concerns about the challenges of achieving stable, localized updates. This aligns with broader skeptical perspectives on the feasibility of treating large language models (LLMs) as reliably editable knowledge repositories (Pinter & Elhadad, 2023). We therefore adopt the approximate stability of principal component directions uk as a working hypothesis to describe a stable editing regime. 

Principal Component Stability Analysis 

In the context of sequential editing ( T = 1 , 2, . . . , all edits), we extract the principal component matrices U (T ) 

> edit

from the hidden states after each T -th edit and compare them to the original principal component matrix Uorig of the unedited model. To assess the geometric consistency between the top-k principal components of U (T ) 

> edit

and Uorig , we calculate the minimum principal angle θmin between their respective subspaces. To further quantify alignment, we compute the cosine of the minimum angle, cos θmin . A value of cos θmin close to 1 indicates strong alignment and stability, while values approaching 0 suggest near-orthogonality and instability. This analysis is performed across a range of subspace dimensions 

k ∈ { 10 , 50 , 100 , 200 , 500 , 1000 , 2000 }.

B.6. Functional Relevance: Correlation with Editing Performance 

This subsection examines whether perturbation amplification is functionally associated with practical editing degradation. Rather than treating amplification at the individual layer level, we evaluate whether step-level summaries of the layer-wise 

Rk distribution correlate with step-level editing performance metrics. 

Step-level Rk Summaries. For each editing method and edit step, we first summarize the layer-wise Rk distribution using several scalar features, including the median, upper quantiles (q90 and q95), and trimmed mean. Because the editing metrics are defined at the step level rather than the layer level, we then aggregate these layer-wise summaries across edited layers to obtain a single step-level amplification statistic. To test the robustness of the observed trends, we consider three cross-layer aggregation strategies: mean, max, and median. 

Correlation Protocol. We measure the association between step-level statistics derived from Rk and editing performance using Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient. We use Spearman correlation because the number of valid edit steps is limited for several methods and the relationship between amplification and performance degradation is not necessarily linear. We exclude MEND from this analysis because its performance under sequential editing degrades to zero too quickly, leaving insufficient variation for a meaningful correlation analysis. 

Main Findings. Table 2 reports the main specification, using the median of the layer-wise Rk distribution followed by mean aggregation across edited layers. Under this default setting, ROME and MEMIT exhibit consistently strong negative correlations between amplification and multiple editing metrics, especially reliability, locality, and portability. WISE shows weaker but still partially aligned negative trends, while AlphaEdit exhibits a qualitatively different pattern: its correlations are generally weak across metrics, possibly because its null-space-constrained update restricts parameter changes to directions designed to preserve existing knowledge and reduce interference. These observations indicate that larger amplification is systematically associated with worse editing outcomes for several parameter-based editing methods, although the strength and direction of this association remain method-dependent.

LLMs. We primarily conduct experiments on decoder-only LLMs, including general-purpose LLMs like Llama-2-7B-Chat (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Meta AI, 2024), and Mistral-7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023). To examine the impact of knowledge editing methods on reasoning-oriented models, we additionally evaluate DeepSeek-R1-Distill-LLaMA-8B (Guo et al., 2025). We also include larger LLMs, Llama-2-13B and Qwen3-14B, for scale comparison. 

Knowledge Editing Methods. This study examines recent knowledge editing methods, which can be broadly categorized based on whether they modify a model’s internal parameters. Parameter-based editing methods include locate-then-edit methods such as ROME (Meng et al., 2022b), MEMIT (Meng et al.), PMET (Li et al., 2024c), RECT (Gu et al., 2024b), AlphaEdit (Fang et al., 2025a), and FT-L (Meng et al., 2022b), as well as meta-learning-based methods such as MEND (De Cao et al., 2021). Additional-parameter methods include AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023b) and WISE (Wang et al., 2024b). In contrast, external memory-based methods include GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2024) and a simple, RAG baseline, Selective Contextual Reasoning (SCR) (He et al., 2025). 

Knowledge Editing Datasets. We use two widely adopted context-free question answering (QA) datasets in knowledge editing research: WikiData counterfact (Cohen et al., 2024) and Zero-Shot Relation Extraction (ZsRE) (Levy et al., 2017), both of which serve as counterfactual benchmarks for modifying knowledge in LLMs. In addition, we incorporate an event-centric dataset, ELKEN (Peng et al., 2024), in which each instance contains multiple related statements, to evaluate the ability of knowledge editing methods to handle more abundant and complex knowledge.

Inference Setting of Knowledge Editing. Following methods such as MEND (Mitchell et al., 2021) and ROME (Meng et al., 2022a), several follow-up studies (Zhang et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024b) have adopted teacher forcing (Williams & Zipser, 1989) during inference and evaluated token-level changes. While this evaluation setting is widely used, it relies on ground-truth answer sequences at inference time, which introduces potential data leakage. Such setups may lead to overly optimistic results and fail to accurately reflect the method’s effectiveness in real-world generative scenarios, as also highlighted by Yang et al. (2025). To ensure a more fair and realistic evaluation, we adopt a unified autoregressive generation paradigm (McCoy et al., 2023) for prediction. We directly evaluate whether the generated answer is correct with respect to the target answer. 

Sequential Editing Scenario. Since single editing is less representative of real-world applications, we conduct experiments under a sequential editing scenario. For each dataset, we edit each knowledge item in sequence, treating the final LLM as the fully edited version. 

Event Knowledge Editing Settings. For parameter-based knowledge editing methods, we use GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024) to convert each event into a set of fact triples, which are then used for sequential editing. The prompt used is given in Appendix C.2. For external memory-based methods, the original event text is directly provided as context input or integrated into the editable memory. 

Implementation Environment. The main experiments are executed on 8 NVIDIA A800 GPUs under a Linux system. All methods are implemented using EasyEdit. 2 Notably, AlphaEdit fails to work with Llama2 and Mistral. We also tested with its original implementation. The results are consistent with those obtained using the EasyEdit version. See Appendix C.6 for further details. Evaluation on reasoning benchmarks is conducted using LUFFY. 3

Metrics Calculation. For the instruct LLM, we limit the max output token length to 50, while for the reasoning LLM, it is set to 1024. For the four evaluation dimensions, we use Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct to assess whether the answers generated by the edited LLMs are semantically consistent with the ground-truth answers, and compute their accuracy (%) accordingly. We validate this evaluation protocol against human judgments on a subset of examples. Manual verification on 250 randomly sampled cases yields 96% agreement with human annotations, supporting the reliability of the automatic evaluation. To evaluate the impact of different editing loads on the performance of knowledge editing, we test scenarios involving 1, 10, and 100 edits. Specifically, we select the first 100 knowledge items from the dataset. For the 1-edit setting, each item is edited individually, and the average performance across all 100 edits is reported. For the 10-edit setting, the 100 items are evenly divided into 10 groups, with sequential editing applied within each group; the average performance after each 10-edit sequence is used. For the 100-edit setting, we sequentially edit all 100 items. Regarding locality, we additionally conduct a token-level validation, which yields results consistent with those at the semantic level (see Appendix C.5).

C.2. Dataset 

• ZsRE : Originally a question-answering dataset, ZsRE is extended by (Yao et al.) to assess various dimensions of model editing methods. • WikiData counterfact : This dataset collects triplets about popular entities, ensuring that the subject corresponds to one of the most viewed Wikipedia pages. • ELKEN : This dataset is designed for event-level knowledge editing, focusing on directly editing new events into LLMs. It includes a diverse set of events and corresponding questions about factual knowledge and future trends. Due to the fact that tendency predictions are influenced by factors beyond the current edited event, we have filtered out questions related to future trends, focusing exclusively on factual knowledge. The first two datasets, ZsRE and WikiData counterfact , are from KnowEdit 4 and released under the MIT License . The 

ELKEN dataset 5 follows the CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 license.

Please extract one or more (subject, relation, object) triples from the following event. Each triple must express a complete, standalone fact, with no redundancy or dependency on other triples. 

Instructions: 

If the event contains multiple independent facts, extract multiple triples , one per fact. The Subject must be the main entity or actor involved in the fact. The Relation should be a clear predicate phrase (verb or action-based phrase) that uniquely points to the Object .The Object should contain the new or important information not repeated in the subject or relation. Avoid vague or generic relations like “is involved in”, “is associated with”. Do not merge multiple facts into a single triple. If there are multiple pieces of information ( e.g. , place, time, role), consider extracting multiple triples .

C.6. AlphaEdit Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis 

During our evaluation, we observed that the performance of AlphaEdit was highly inconsistent across different LLM architectures. While it performed reasonably well on LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct, its performance on Llama-2-7B-Chat and Mistral-7B-Instruct was significantly worse, often approaching zero in sequential editing scenarios. 6 This appendix details our investigation into the hyperparameter sensitivity of AlphaEdit. 

Experimental Investigation. We conducted experiments using the authors’ latest original implementation from the AlphaEdit GitHub repository. All experiments were performed on a single H20 (96 GB) GPU. We used the same settings as MEMIT for a shared setup and focused on AlphaEdit’s unique hyperparameters: nullspace thresholdand the L2 regularization coefficient. We fixed the nullspace thresholdat 2e-2 and experimented with two different values for the L2 coefficient: 1 and 10. 

Results. Table 41 present the results for single and sequential editing across different models. The data reveal a high degree of sensitivity to both hyperparameters and model architecture. On Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, performance varies significantly with the L2 coefficient. More critically, on Llama-2-7B-Chat and Mistral, AlphaEdit’s performance is near-zero in sequential editing scenarios regardless of the hyperparameter values tested. This indicates a fundamental incompatibility or instability of the method with these model architectures under sequential editing. 

Conclusion. Our analysis demonstrates that AlphaEdit exhibits significant hyperparameter sensitivity and model-specific performance. The method’s inability to achieve non-trivial results on certain widely-used LLMs (Llama-2, Mistral) under sequential editing highlights a critical limitation for its practical applicability.

## D. Fairness of SCR vs. Parameter-Based Editing Methods 

In this study, we primarily treat SCR as a reference baseline rather than a direct competitor to parameter-based knowledge editing methods. By operating outside the parametric editing paradigm, SCR provides a distinct empirical perspective for analyzing the trade-offs between efficiency and reliability in knowledge editing. To further explore the comparative advantages and limitations of SCR versus parameter-based editing approaches, we conducted two complementary analyses using LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct and the ZsRE dataset. 

Evaluation of Efficiency and Reliability 

As summarized in Table 42, we compare SCR and the parameter-based editing method AlphaEdit across multiple metrics, including edit time, inference latency, reliability, and locality. Evaluations are conducted at two sequential-editing scales (N = 100 and N = 1000). The results highlight key operational differences between the two methods. SCR exhibits near-zero upfront editing cost by shifting computational effort to retrieval-based inference, at the expense of retrieval latency. Conversely, AlphaEdit achieves faster results during inference but incurs significant computational cost during initial parameter modification. 

Integration of SCR Retrieval for Parameter-Based Editing Methods 

To ensure a fair comparison, we augment parameter-based editing methods with an SCR-inspired retrieval mechanism. Using sequential editing scales (N = 10, 100, 500, 1000, all ) and a top-3 retrieval strategy, results (Table 43) reveal that SCR consistently outperforms baseline parameter-based editing methods across key metrics. These findings highlight the inherent limitations of direct parameter editing. Moreover, integrating SCR with parameter-based editing methods enhances their overall performance, demonstrating the efficacy of leveraging external contextual knowledge through retrieval.
