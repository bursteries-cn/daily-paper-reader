## Knowledge Editing in Masked Diffusion Language Models 

Haewon Park Yohan Jo †

Graduate School of Data Science, Seoul National University 

{dellaanima2, yohan.jo}@snu.ac.kr 

Abstract 

Knowledge editing aims to update or correct factual knowledge in a language model. Awidely used approach, locate-then-edit , does this in two steps: it first localizes a fact within the model, then edits the weights there. To date, such methods have been developed exclusively on autoregressive models (ARMs). Whether their underlying assumptions hold for masked diffusion models (MDMs), which model text bidirectionally and generate by iterative denois-ing rather than next-token prediction, remains an open question. We address it by transfer-ring locate-then-edit to MDMs and comparing two MDMs (LLaDA, Dream) with two ARMs (LLaMA, Qwen) at matched scale. Our cen-tral finding has two parts. First, where an edit is applied transfers across paradigms: causal tracing highlights the same early-to-mid-layer MLP at the last subject token in both, and edit-ing is most effective there. Second, this shared location does not guarantee a shared outcome. Single-token edits succeed in both, but as tar-gets grow longer, editing degrades systemat-ically in the MDMs but not the ARMs. The failure stems from how the edited fact is gen-erated: producing a multi-token target requires passing through partially unmasked interme-diate states for which the edit was never opti-mized. Guided by this diagnosis, we introduce a simple correction that optimizes the edit for these states, substantially restoring multi-token performance. 1

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) acquire extensive factual knowledge through pretraining, but not all of it is correct: some facts are simply wrong, a prod-uct of noise in the training data, and others grow stale over time (Ji et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2022). over the full model is computationally expensive and risks overfitting to the correction while eroding unrelated knowledge through catastrophic forget-ting (Bethune et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2020). Knowledge editing offers an alterna-tive, revising targeted facts while keeping the rest of the model’s knowledge intact (Yao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b). A fact is typically represented as a tuple (s, r, o )—a subject s, a relation r, and an object o—and an edit replaces the object with a new target object o∗ for a given (s, r ): for example, “Lionel Messi (s) plays for (r) FC Barcelona (o)”can be edited to “Inter Miami” (o∗). Among such methods, the locate-then-edit approach identifies where a fact is causally localized by analyzing the model’s internals, then modifies only the weights at that location (Meng et al., 2023a,b). Because the edit is grounded in an explicit analysis rather than distributed across the whole model or delegated to an auxiliary network (De Cao et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2022a,b), the edit site is interpretable. These methods, however, were developed and validated entirely on autoregressive models (ARMs). Masked diffusion models (MDMs) (Nie et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025) have recently emerged as a competitive alterna-tive paradigm, yet knowledge editing for MDMs remains largely unexplored. Whether locate-then-edit, designed for ARMs, also applies to MDMs is the question this work takes up. The challenge runs deeper than reimplementation: the location these methods modify is not arbitrary but derived from how ARMs work. Using causal tracing, ROME (Meng et al., 2023a) localized a fact’s recall to the early-to-mid-layer MLP of the last subject token, read this MLP as a form of associative mem-ory, and made it the edit target; MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023b) built on the same site to scale to many edits at once. This grounding, however, comes en-tirely from ARMs, which differ from MDMs even in how knowledge is acquired: an ARM is trained 1

to predict the next token, whereas an MDM recov-ers masked tokens from bidirectional context. It is therefore not obvious that an edit site identified in ARMs carries over to a model trained so differently. To investigate this, we transfer locate-then-edit to MDMs without altering its algorithm, apply-ing only the adjustments needed for the MDM’s masked prediction (§3.3), and we compare ARMs (LLaMA, Qwen) and MDMs (LLaDA, Dream) un-der matched scale. We examine the question at two levels: we perform causal tracing to locate where facts are causally localized, and we measure edit-ing performance across token positions and layer ranges to identify where editing is most effective. We find that even in MDMs, where every token attends to the full subject bidirectionally, the local-ization of facts coincides with that in ARMs—the early-to-mid-layer MLP of the last subject token. The location assumption underlying locate-then-edit is thus not specific to autoregressive training but holds across both paradigms. Sharing a location, however, does not mean shar-ing an outcome. While the two paradigms perform comparably when the target object is a single to-ken, the MDM degrades substantially once the tar-get spans multiple tokens. The cause lies not in the location but in the generation process. Locate-then-edit fits the edit on a single state. An ARM passes through exactly that state as it decodes left to right, so the edit operates under the conditions it was fit for. An MDM, by contrast, starts from a fully masked output and unmasks its positions over several denoising steps, passing through a chain of partial-mask states the edit was never optimized for. As a result, editing degrades systematically as the target spans more tokens. Building on this diagno-sis, we propose a simple correction that makes the edit robust to these intermediate states, and show that it substantially restores multi-token editing in MDMs. Our contributions are as follows: • We present the first analysis of locate-then-edit knowledge editing in MDMs (§3), show-ing that its core location assumption—that recall is mediated by the early-to-mid-layer MLP at the last subject token—holds across both paradigms. • We find that in MDMs, locate-then-edit suf-fers a multi-token breakdown (§4): edit-ing succeeds for single-token target objects but degrades systematically as the target ob-ject grows longer, which we trace to a mis-match between single-location editing and it-erative denoising. Standard benchmarks, dom-inated by single-token targets, cannot reveal this, so we surface and analyze the breakdown with a multi-token evaluation adapted from KAMEL (Kalo and Fichtel, 2022). • We propose a simple correction (§4.3) that makes the edit robust to the partial-mask states traversed during generation, substan-tially restoring multi-token editing perfor-mance in MDMs. 

2 Preliminaries 

2.1 Locate-then-Edit Knowledge Editing 

Knowledge editing aims to modify a fact triplet from (s, r, o ) to (s, r, o ∗). Locate-then-edit meth-ods (Meng et al., 2023a,b) achieve this by view-ing the down-projection layer W of a Transformer MLP as a linear associative memory (Anderson, 1972; Kohonen, 1972) that maps a key vector k to a value vector v via Wk = v. The key k, taken as the MLP up-projection activation at the last to-ken of the subject s, encodes the entity, while the value v encodes the associated (r, o ). Editing a fact amounts to adding an update ∆ to W so that the same key maps to a new value v∗ encoding (r, o ∗),i.e., (W + ∆)k ≈ v∗. This choice of where to edit rests on causal tracing (Meng et al., 2023a), which identifies the components most responsible for recalling a fact—the early-to-mid-layer MLPs at the last subject token. Building on this site, MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023b) edits a contiguous range of these MLP lay-ers and, for a batch of facts, solves for the update ∆

in closed form from the keys k and the correspond-ing target values v∗ (full derivation in Appendix A). While the key k is read directly from the subject, the target value v∗ is obtained by optimization: it is the value that, when written at the edit location, drives the model to produce the target object o∗

given the prompt p describing (s, r ). Concretely,

Second, the model is trained to reconstruct masked tokens rather than to predict the next one, so the factual knowledge that locate-then-edit modifies is formed under a learning objective different from that of ARMs. We study two MDMs that realize this objective differently: LLaDA is trained from scratch, whereas Dream is initialized from the weights of an ARM (Qwen2.5) and trained with a shifted objective that aligns its prediction with that of an ARM (Ye et al., 2025). 

Iterative denoising. Unlike AR decoding, which produces tokens one at a time, an MDM gener-ates by iterative denoising . Starting from a fully masked output of length L, at each step the model predicts all masked positions, unmasks a subset of them (e.g., the most confident ones), and leaves the rest masked for subsequent steps (Nie et al., 2025). Producing a multi-token answer therefore unfolds over several steps and necessarily passes through 

intermediate states in which some positions are already filled while others remain masked. As we show in §4, these intermediate states—which have no counterpart in AR generation—are where an edit optimized for a single conditioning state can fail to take effect. 

3 What Transfers: Locating and Editing Facts 

The locate-then-edit approach proceeds in two stages: causal tracing identifies the components most responsible for recalling a fact, and the weights at those locations are then modified. We test whether each stage transfers to MDMs. After the setup and our MDM adaptation (§3.1), we ap-ply causal tracing to locate where facts are causally localized (§3.2) and measure where editing is most effective across token positions and layers (§3.3). In both, despite the MDM’s bidirectional training, it localizes facts to the same site as the ARMs—the early-to-mid-layer MLP of the last subject token. 

3.1 Setup Models. We study two ARM–MDM pairs matched in parameter count and layer depth, so each comparison controls for model capacity: LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024) (ARM) / LLaDA-8B-Instruct (Nie et al., 2025) (MDM), and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Qwen et al., 2025) (ARM) / Dream-v0-Instruct-7B (Ye et al., 2025) (MDM). The latter is especially controlled, as Dream is initialized from the Qwen2.5 weights. 

Dataset and metrics. We evaluate on C OUNTER -FACT (Meng et al., 2023a), the standard knowledge-editing benchmark, using its three criteria (Meng et al., 2023a,b): efficacy (the rewrite prompt (s, r )

elicits the new target), generalization (paraphrase prompts elicit it too), and specificity (a neighbor-hood prompt, with a distinct subject s′ sharing r

and o, still yields the original object o). Following MEMIT’s batched setting, we edit 500 facts in a single batch. We assess all three from the model’s generation; details are deferred to Appendix B.

3.3 Editing Facts 

Causal tracing (§3.2) reveals where a fact is causally localized; this section asks where editing is actually effective . The two need not coincide— the most causally implicated location is not nec-essarily the most effective one to edit (Hase et al., 2023)—so rather than assuming the tracing result, we measure editing performance directly. We re-port three analyses: how performance varies with the edited token position , with the edited layer , and how robust the edits are under generation settings that reflect the MDM’s decoding process. 

Transferring locate-then-edit to MDMs. We keep the locate-then-edit algorithm (MEMIT) un-changed and apply only the minimal adjustments needed to match the inference behavior of an MDM. Both paradigms optimize the same target value (Eq. 1); they differ only in the conditioning state under which o∗ is scored. The ARM appends the answer tokens directly to the rewriting prompt and reads o∗ as the next-token continuation, as it is trained to do. An MDM in-stead recovers masked positions. We place a block of mask tokens at the answer slot of each rewriting prompt, one for each token of the edit target o∗,and train the edit so that the model reconstructs o∗

there. For instance, to edit the team Messi plays for to “Inter Miami”, the answer slot holds two mask tokens, and the edit is scored on whether the model reconstructs “Inter” and “Miami” jointly. Because an MDM uses bidirectional attention, the mask tokens at the object positions can influence the fact-lookup hidden state even though they ap-pear after the lookup position in the input. Robustness to generation settings. Unlike an ARM, which decodes one token at a time from left to right, an MDM fills a fully masked output over a number of denoising steps, so its genera-tion depends on both the generation length and the number of steps. Since the edit is optimized once at the target length (§3.3), a natural concern is whether it holds under other settings. We therefore take the edited model as is and vary the generation length and step count over {8, 16 , 32 } at evaluation (Table 1). Performance is stable: efficacy stays near 0.85 for LLaDA and 0.90 for Dream across all settings, with generalization and specificity sim-ilarly steady. The edit thus holds across decoding configurations it was not tuned for. 

4 What Breaks: Editing Multi-Token Facts

That success, however, was measured on C OUNTER FACT , whose targets are almost al-ways a single token. The edit is optimized for one state—the fully masked answer. A single-token target is unmasked in one step from exactly this state, so the edit applies as intended. The editing results of §3.3 were measured on COUNTER FACT , whose targets are overwhelm-ingly single-token (Table 2). 4.2 The Edit Is Not Optimized for Intermediate States 

To understand where this failure originates, we examine whether the model fails because it does not know the right tokens of the target object or because it cannot assemble them in the right po-sitions. We measure two rates: how often each target token (i.e., each token in the target object) appears anywhere in the generation, and how often the complete target is produced (Table 5). The first is much higher than the second—roughly half the target tokens surface somewhere, yet far fewer full targets are generated eventually. As described in §2.1, MEMIT optimizes a single residual δ so that the model produces the target from one state: the fully masked answer. But an MDM generates a multi-token target over sev-eral steps rather than from that single state (§2.2). It unmasks one position at a time, passing through intermediate states in which some positions are al-ready filled while others remain masked—states δ

was never optimized for. And because the MDM is bidirectional, each position it unmasks becomes context that reshapes the predictions at the posi-tions still masked. A single token can therefore be recovered in isolation, which is why the right tokens do appear; but the moment one is unmasked, it shifts the conditions for the rest, under which 

δ was never fit. Over a multi-token target these shifts compound—the more positions, the longer the chain and the wider the gap we observe. This explains our earlier observations. A single-token target has only one position and no interme-diate state, so the edit operates exactly where it was optimized—which is why single-token edits were comparable for both ARMs and MDMs. Longer targets traverse more intermediate states, where failures accumulate. The ARM never faces this mismatch, as it generates left to right in one pass. The failure thus stems not from where the edit is applied but from how the edited fact is generated, and is specific to the iterative denoising of MDMs. 74.3 A Correction for Intermediate States 

The residual δ (Eq. 1) is optimized only on the fully masked state, yet inference visits that state once and spends the rest of generation on partially unmasked ones. If this is the cause, the remedy is to fit δ on those intermediate states as well. We broaden only the inputs on which δ is fit, leaving the update rule and edit layers as they are. During optimization we vary how many of the N target to-kens are already unmasked, cycling through every count from none to N −1, and at each step we re-sample which positions are revealed. Sweeping the count this way exposes δ to the same progression generation runs through, from the fully masked answer, through each partially unmasked stage, to the verge of completion, rather than to the masked state alone. It thus learns to recover the target from each state inference may encounter. Resampling the revealed positions, rather than fixing an order, keeps δ from overfitting to one unmasking pattern; schedules and ablations are in Appendix I. This single change substantially fills the multi-token cliff (Table 6). Both efficacy and gener-alization rise across every target length and for both models—LLaDA’s efficacy at N =4 roughly triples, from 0.27 to 0.73 , and Dream’s general-ization at N =2 climbs from 0.46 to 0.81 . Every-thing else in the locate-then-edit procedure of §2.1 is unchanged—the closed-form update, the edited layers, the key and target value—and only the con-ditioning states in Eq. 1 are broadened from the sin-gle masked state to include the intermediate ones. That so narrow a change recovers a large part of the drop suggests the intermediate-state mismatch is an important factor, consistent with our diagnosis. 

5 Related Work 

Knowledge Editing. Knowledge editing meth-ods fall into three paradigms. Memory-based ap-proaches store edited knowledge externally and retrieve it at inference, leaving the model un-touched (Mitchell et al., 2022b; Hartvigsen et al., 2023). Meta-learning approaches train auxiliary networks to predict weight updates (De Cao et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2022a). Locate-then-edit ap-proaches instead identify the parameters associated with a fact and modify them directly (Meng et al., 2023a,b). This last family has been especially in-fluential: by grounding the edit in a causal analysis of where a fact is localized, it makes the edit site interpretable, and has since scaled to many simulta-neous edits while reducing side effects (Fang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024; Park et al., 2026; Gupta et al., 2024). We take it as our starting point both for its strong performance at scale and because its edit location is derived from an explicit analysis of the model. This makes it well suited for asking whether such an analysis, developed on ARMs, carries over to MDMs. 

ARMs vs. MDMs. A growing body of work com-pares the two paradigms. Some studies contrast their capabilities, finding advantages of MDMs in knowledge acquisition (Pan et al., 2026) or differ-ences in long-context behavior (Liu et al., 2025). Closer to our setting, Piskorz et al. (2025) show that appended mask tokens distract context com-prehension and propose a mask-agnostic loss, and Wang et al. (2026) interpret MDM representations through sparse features. These analyses operate on features or inference-time behavior; none ad-dresses where a fact resides in the weights or how to edit it, and where they modify training objec-tives or activations, we leave the model’s training untouched and intervene only on the edit. To our knowledge, ours is the first work to bring locate-then-edit knowledge editing, developed on ARMs, to MDMs, and to compare the two paradigms from this perspective. 

6 Conclusion 

We transferred locate-then-edit knowledge editing from ARMs to MDMs. The two agree on where 

an edit applies: facts concentrate at the early-to-mid-layer MLP of the last subject token in both, and editing there matches the ARM on standard benchmarks. They diverge on how the edited fact is generated. Because such benchmarks are almost entirely single-token, this divergence is invisible on them; evaluating across target lengths reveals that MDM editing degrades as the target grows, since iterative denoising visits intermediate states the edit was never optimized for. Including those states in the optimization restores most of the lost performance, confirming the cause; a gap at the longest targets remains for future work. Editing an MDM thus turns not only on where a fact is placed but on how it is generated—porting an edit-ing method across architectures means matching the generation process, not only the site it acts on. 8Limitations 

We instantiate locate-then-edit with MEMIT, the most established method of its kind. Other meth-ods in this family share the same locate-and-edit structure and could plausibly transfer to MDMs in the same way, though we have not verified this. Our analysis also covers two ARM–MDM pairs at a single scale. We chose these pairs because each matches an MDM to an ARM of the same archi-tecture and size, which isolates the effect of the training objective—the cleanest such pairings cur-rently available; whether the findings hold at larger scales or for other MDM families remains open. Finally, we identify and address one cause of multi-token failure—the mismatch between the single state the edit is optimized for and the intermediate states generation visits. This accounts for most of the degradation but not all of it: our correction ex-poses the edit to how many positions are unmasked, but not to which positions or in what order, and a gap remains at the longest targets. Closing it may require accounting for the order in which an MDM unmasks its output, which we leave to future work. 

Ethics Statement 

Knowledge editing modifies the facts a model produces, and the same techniques that correct outdated or erroneous knowledge can in princi-ple be used to insert false or misleading informa-tion. Our work studies whether and how locate-then-edit transfers to masked diffusion models, rather than proposing editing for deployment, and our experiments use standard public benchmarks (C OUNTER FACT , K AMEL ) and openly available models. We nonetheless note that editing methods should be applied with appropriate safeguards— such as provenance tracking and human oversight— particularly as they extend to new model families. We see no additional risks specific to our analysis beyond those already present in the knowledge-editing literature.

This appendix expands the transfer described in §3.3 to the level of the implementation. The closed-form solver, the key/value framework, and the delta optimization loop of MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023b) are kept intact; the only change is that every in-put distribution on which MEMIT runs a forward pass is mask-augmented to match the bidirectional attention of an MDM. A single MEMIT edit runs forward passes on four distinct input distributions. In the ARM case all of them use the raw prompt (or prompt followed by the target); in the MDM case we put all of them in the same form, a prompt followed by a block of mask tokens at the answer slot, with one mask token per token of the edit target o∗.1. Rewriting prompts. The main term of Eq. 1: we append the mask block to each fact’s rewriting prompt and supervise the logits at the mask positions toward o∗. The ARM’s next-token logits at the final answer positions thus map onto the logits read at the mask po-sitions. 2. KL-anchor prompt. Beyond the term shown in Eq. 1, the optimization also includes a KL regularizer that keeps unrelated predictions stable. Its anchor is likewise mask-augmented: in place of the standard subject-last next-token logits, we read the logits at the mask position of the anchor prompt followed by a mask to-ken. 3. Key extraction. The key k for each fact is read as the subject-last hidden state on the mask-augmented prompt. 4. Residual baseline. The baseline value used to form the closed-form residual is likewise read from the subject-last hidden state on the mask-augmented prompt. Under the causal attention of an ARM, the subject-last hidden state is identical on the raw prompt and on the prompt followed by the target, since future tokens cannot be attended to; the two hidden states that MEMIT reads—the optimization target and the residual baseline—then coincide automatically. Under the bidirectional attention of an MDM this coincidence breaks, so we force all four distribu-tions to be defined over the same masked context. 

Hyperparameters. The two ARMs follow the MEMIT settings provided in EasyEdit (Wang et al., 2024a): learning rate 10 −1, 25 gradient steps for the target optimization, clamp-norm factor 0.75 ,and KL factor 0.0625 . For the two MDMs, we inherit the hyperparameters of their architectural sibling without retuning: LLaDA from LLaMA (both share the LLaMA-style transformer block) and Dream from Qwen (Dream’s backbone is Qwen2.5). The closed-form solver, the covariance term, the weight update, and the distribution of the residual across layers are all as in the original MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023b). The only setting we determine per model is the edit layer , chosen by a direct layer sweep that selects the best 4-layer window by the rank-sum of efficacy and general-ization; this yields LLaMA-3 L 1–4, LLaDA L 4–7,Qwen2.5 L 7–10 , and Dream L 3–6 (Appendix E). 

E Editing Layer Selection 

The edit layer is the one setting we tune per model, since editing performance is sensitive to which MLP layers are modified and prior defaults do not transfer reliably across our four models. For each model we sweep every 4-layer contiguous window on a held-out subset of C OUNTER FACT 

(500 facts), score each window by the rank-sum of its efficacy and generalization ranks, and se-lect the best-scoring window (ties broken by effi-cacy). We exclude specificity from the criterion because it trades off against efficacy: the highest-specificity windows tend to under-edit. The sweep uses the same evaluation settings as the main ex-periments (Appendix B) and the hyperparameters of Appendix D.

I Partial-Mask Augmentation: Details and Ablations 

This appendix specifies the partial-mask augmenta-tion of §4.3 and ablates its two design choices: how often each partially unmasked state is visited dur-ing optimization, and how the revealed positions are chosen. 

I.1 Method 

The correction broadens the states on which the residual δ (Eq. 1) is fit, leaving everything else in MEMIT unchanged. For a target of N tokens, at op-timization step t we reveal k = t mod N of the tar-get positions, cycling through k = 0 , 1, . . . , N −1

over training. At each step we pick k positions, fill them with the corresponding target tokens, mask the rest, and update δ on the N −k still-masked positions. The fully masked state ( k=0 ) and ev-ery partially unmasked state thus appear in turn, matching the progression generation runs through. 

I.2 Ablation 1: Which states to visit 

We fix everything except how the count k is dis-tributed across optimization, varying only which partially unmasked states are emphasized (Table 11, LLaDA on K AMEL ). The largest gain comes simply from adding aug-mentation at all: moving from (a) to any aug-mented variant lifts efficacy by roughly 25 –30 per-centage points (pp) across lengths. Among the augmented variants, emphasizing the harder, less-revealed states (b) beats emphasizing the easier ones (d), and the gap grows with N . Visiting every count equally, whether at random (c) or in a fixed cycle (e), is best overall, with the deterministic cy-cle slightly ahead at N =3 , 4. We adopt the cycle as our default.

J Computational resources. 

All experiments were run on a single NVIDIA H200 GPU (144GB). The models we study have 15 7–8B parameters (§3.1). Editing time scales with the number of denoising steps and is roughly con-stant per sample: for LLaDA, a single edit takes about 8.7 seconds per fact (e.g., ∼29 minutes for 200 facts), and for Dream about 4.5 seconds per fact ( ∼15 minutes for 200 facts). A full edit of 500 facts therefore takes approximately 70 minutes for LLaDA and 40 minutes for Dream on one GPU. 16
