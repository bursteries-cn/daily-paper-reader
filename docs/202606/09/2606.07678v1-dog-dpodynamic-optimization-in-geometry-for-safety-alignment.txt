Abstract 

Safety alignment for large language models re-lies on preference data, but current pipelines often train on large, redundant datasets. Ex-isting data selection methods typically score each preference pair independently, collaps-ing directional preference information into scalar quality or diversity scores. This sample-centric view is especially limiting in multi-dataset settings, where shared safety directions coexist with dataset-specific residual risks. We propose DOG-DPO , a training-free data se-lection framework that treats preference pairs as structured geometric signals. DOG-DPO first represents each preference pair as a di-rection in model representation space. It then decomposes multi-dataset preference geome-try into a global anchor subspace and dataset-specific residual subspaces. Finally, it se-lects subsets by maximizing diversity-based coverage, encouraging broad, non-redundant coverage of alignment directions before DPO training. Across six safety benchmarks and two model backbones, DOG-DPO achieves a strong utility‚Äďrobustness trade-off using only 11% of the preference pairs. It recovers most of the safety gains of full-data training while remaining entirely teacher-free, training-free, and substantially faster than representative se-lection baselines.

1 Introduction 

Aligning large language models (LLMs) with safety preferences has become a central problem in modern AI systems (Lu et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2026). Most alignment pipelines rely on large-scale human feedback, typically in the form of preference pairs used in reward modeling or Direct Preference Op-timization (DPO) (Kim et al., 2025; Shang et al., 2025). While these approaches achieve strong empirical performance, they are inherently data-intensive (Bai et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022): collecting and training on large safety datasets is costly, noisy, and often inefficient. Recent stud-ies suggest that only a small fraction of alignment data is truly necessary, indicating substantial re-dundancy in existing datasets (Yang et al., 2025b; Gao et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024a; Bukharin et al., 2024). This raises a fundamental question: how should we select informative subsets of preference data for efficient alignment? While prior work shows that careful data selection can match or even outperform full-dataset training, the underly-ing principles of selecting preference data remain poorly understood‚ÄĒespecially in realistic settings where data comes from multiple heterogeneous sources (Liu et al., 2024b; Chung et al., 2024). Existing data selection methods approach this problem from several perspectives, including dis-tributional diversity (Yang et al., 2025b; Bukharin et al., 2024), training-signal analysis (Gao et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025), and heuristic scoring (Liu et al., 2024a,b). While effective in their respective settings, these approaches are primar-ily designed for generic data selection and do not explicitly account for the structured nature of preference data. In particular, they adopt a sample-centric view that scores or ranks each pref-erence pair independently, collapsing its informa-tion into a scalar. This view loses two struc-tural properties that are intrinsic to preference data. 

First, preference signals are inherently direc-tional rather than pointwise : a pair (x, y +, y ‚ąí)

does not just label two candidates‚ÄĒit specifies the alignment direction along which the model should shift. Sample-level scoring discards this direction-ality. Second, in multi-dataset settings, prefer-ence data carries cross-dataset structure : some alignment directions are well captured by a domi-nant anchor dataset, while others remain dataset-specific and reflect heterogeneous risk domains

(Liu et al., 2024a; Gao et al., 2025). Methods that pool preference pairs into a single homogeneous candidate set cannot distinguish these two regimes, leading to redundant signals on anchor-aligned directions and undercoverage of dataset-specific ones. A third practical limitation compounds the conceptual ones: training-signal-based methods require auxiliary forward/backward passes or ref-erence DPO runs to extract their selection sig-nal, making them computationally expensive and tightly coupled to the training process (Gao et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). In this work, we depart from the sample-centric view and treat preference data as a structured ge-ometric object in representation space. Each pref-erence pair is represented by the difference vec-tor between preferred and rejected response em-beddings, yielding a directional alignment signal rather than a scalar-scored sample. Across V

preference datasets, these directions contain both a dominant anchor geometry and dataset-specific residual variations. This turns data selection into a geometric coverage problem: selecting non-redundant directions that span both shared and residual preference structure. We instantiate this view as DOG-DPO , a training-free framework that builds anchor/residual coordinates and selects subsets using a determinant-based diversity crite-rion. It requires no teacher LLM, no reference DPO run, and no iterative validation. We evaluate DOG-DPO on six safety alignment benchmarks across two backbones. Using only 11.6% of the preference pairs, DOG-DPO remains competitive with full-data training, recovers most safety gains while maintaining general LLM capa-bilities, reduces inter-dataset selection bias, and expands feature-space coverage relative to base-lines. These results suggest that effective align-ment depends not only on data scale, but also on exploiting the structured geometry of heteroge-neous preference data. We summarize our contri-butions as follows: ‚ÄĘ A geometric reformulation of preference data. 

Departing from the prevailing sample-centric view, we represent each preference pair as a di-rectional signal in representation space and re-veal that, across heterogeneous sources, these signals decompose into a shared anchor geom-etry and dataset-specific residual variations. ‚ÄĘ A geometry-driven selection framework. 

Building on this view, we propose DOG-DPO ,which selects subsets by maximizing a diversity-based coverage objective over the induced fea-ture space, jointly capturing structural breadth and directional non-redundancy. ‚ÄĘ Training-free selection. DOG-DPO requires no teacher LLM, reference DPO training, or iterative validation, running as a single pre-processing pass and achieving 15‚Äď35 √ó speedups over representative baselines (Figure 3). ‚ÄĘ Strong safety-alignment results. Across six benchmarks and two backbones, DOG-DPO uses only 11.6% of the preference pairs while re-maining competitive with full-data training and improving selection balance and feature-space coverage over existing baselines.

3 Experiment 

3.1 Datasets 

We evaluate our method on a diverse set of safety and robustness benchmarks, covering both stan-dard alignment metrics and adversarial attack sce-narios. Unless otherwise noted, the representation extractor is the same frozen backbone as the down-stream DPO model used in that experiment. 

Benchmarks. We evaluate on five robustness benchmarks ‚Äď AutoDAN-HGA (Liu et al., 2023) (genetic-search jailbreak over 520 AdvBench prompts), WildJailbreak (Jiang et al., 2024) (200 harmful), JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024) (100 prompts across 10 harm categories), Harm-Bench (Mazeika et al., 2024) (200 prompts, both keyword and GPT-judged ASR) and two utility benchmarks: TruthfulQA-MC2 (Lin et al., 2022) and ToxiGen (Hartvigsen et al., 2022). Together, these benchmarks cover both utility (truthfulness and safety compliance) and robustness (jailbreak resistance). The union of all five preference datasets contains 260k preference pairs in total; the K=30k budget used in our main experiments therefore corresponds to 11% of the pool. Baselines. We compare DOG-D against repre-sentative baselines spanning the main families of preference-data selection: the unaligned backbone (Base ); Random sampling; distribution-based clus-tering with out feature vectors ( KMeans ); gradi-ent stratified sampling based: ( Laser (Mirza et al., 2025)); LLM-judge scoring ( DEITA (Liu et al., 2024a)); reward-model reject sampling ( RM-RS ); training-based selection ( SDPO (Gao et al., 2025), which uses a reference DPO run to filter unstable examples); and full-data DPO on all 259k pairs without selection ( Whole ). To ensure a fair comparison, we separate base-lines into tiers according to their computational requirements relative to our training-free setting. Tier 1 contains the unaligned Base model and Ran-dom selection. Tier 2 contains training-free selec-tion methods that operate at comparable compute to ours (KMeans, Laser, DEITA, RM-RS). Tier 3 contains methods that require either a full DPO training pass before selection (SDPO) or use the entire dataset without selection (Whole), incurring 15‚Äď35 √ó the compute of Tier-2 methods (Figure 3). We report Tier-3 results as upper-bound references rather than directly comparable baselines under a fair compute budget. 

3.2 Evaluation Evaluation Metrics. We group benchmarks into 

utility and robustness (ASR) . For each trained model we compute three aggregate scores from the per-benchmark numbers reported in tables 4 and 5 in Appendix. Robustness converts each attack-success rate (ASR m) into a higher-is-better score 1 ‚ąí ASR m and averages over the at-tack benchmarks: AUTODAN,WildJailbreak, Jail-breakBench and HarmBench. Utility averages two higher-is-better axes of general capability. The Overall score is the mean of the two aggregates, balancing safety and helpfulness. 

3.3 Main Result 

Table 1 shows that DOG-D delivers the strongest overall utility‚Äďrobustness trade-off across all Tier 1 and 2 settings while using only 10k or 30k preference pairs. On LLaMA3.2-3B , DOG-D attains the highest aggregate robustness at both budgets ( R=0 .9563 

at 10k, R=0 .9657 at 30k), exceeding the next-best Tier-2 baseline (KMeans at 10k, R=0 .9071 ;DOG ‚Ä° at 30k, R=0 .9608 ) by 4.9 points at 10k, and outperforming the unaligned backbone by over 21 points. It simultaneously achieves the best Tier-2 utility at 30k ( U =0 .5760 ), driven by gains on both TruthfulQA-MC2 ( 0.589 vs. 0.561 

for Laser) and ToxiGen. Per-benchmark break-downs in Table 4 confirm these aggregate gains: DOG-D reaches the lowest AutoDAN-GPT ASR among all Tier-2 methods at both 10k ( 0.0597 )and 30k ( 0.0272 ), and ties the lowest GPT-judged HarmBench ASR at 30k ( 0.0050 ). On Gemma3-1B , DOG-D again leads on ag-gregate robustness at both budgets ( R=0 .7650 

at 10k, R=0 .7725 at 30k) and on utility at 30k (U =0 .4710 ). At 10k, Laser edges DOG-D slightly on utility ( 0.4605 vs. 0.4580 ) but at the cost of lower robustness ( R=0 .7320 vs. 0.7650 ); DOG-D retains the best overall trade-off. These results support our claim that combining anchor/residual structure with feature-level diversity yields subsets that are more informative than their size alone. 

Comparison to Tier-3 references. A small gap to Whole remains on LLaMA3.2-3B at 30k ( R=0 .9657 /U =0 .5760 vs. 0.9768 /0.6025 ), concentrated on TruthfulQA-MC2 (0.5890

3.4 Analysis Runtime efficiency. Beyond selection quality, practical deployment hinges on how expensive the selection pipeline itself is. We therefore benchmark the end-to-end wall-clock cost of our method against representative baselines in Table 3. Quality-scoring approaches such as DEITA (Liu et al., 2024b) are particularly costly: for every can-didate they invoke a 13B scorer LLM twice (once for quality, once for complexity), and each invo-cation is not a single forward pass but a full au-toregressive decoding call that generates a chain-of-thought rationale together with the score token. Gradient-similarity methods like LASER (Mirza et al., 2025) removes the decoding bottleneck by replacing the generative scorer with a regres-sion head, so each candidate only incurs a single LLM forward pass; however, it still routes sam-ples through domain-specific quality scorers and a constraint-verification step for instruction follow-ing, which together amount to roughly 6 GPU-hours on our pool. In contrast, our pipeline only performs a one-shot embedding pass followed by a DPP-based selection whose greedy implementa-tion runs in O(N k 2) time and parallelizes trivially across shards. End-to-end this brings the total cost down to less than 24 minutes.

4 Conclusion 

In this work, we study the problem of data-efficient alignment for large language models and identify a key limitation of existing data selection methods: they do not explicitly model the struc-tured nature of preference data, especially in multi-dataset settings. We argue that preference sig-nals admit a geometric interpretation, where align-ment information is encoded as structured direc-tions in representation space. Based on this view, we propose DOG-DPO, a dynamic and geometry-driven data selection framework that identifies an-chor geometry and dataset-specific residual varia-tions, and selects subsets that maximize geometric coverage via volume expansion. This formulation enables efficient selection without relying on exter-nal supervision or training. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves strong per-formance using significantly less data, while im-proving coverage and reducing redundancy. These results highlight that effective alignment depends not only on data scale, but on understanding the structured geometry of preference data. 5 Limitations 

DOG-DPO is substantially more efficient than training-based or judge-based selection, it still re-quires representation extraction and subset opti-mization over the candidate pool, which can be-come costly at very large scale. Third, our evalua-tion focuses primarily on safety alignment bench-marks. Although the experiment provides ini-tial evidence that DOG-DPO transfers beyond the 1B‚Äď3B full-finetuning setting, broader validation across more model families, larger backbones, and non-safety preference domains remains future work. 

6 Broader Impact 

DOG-DPO can make safety alignment more effi-cient by reducing redundant preference data, low-ering training costs, and decreasing dependence on expensive teacher or judge models. By select-ing preference pairs through multi-dataset geomet-ric coverage, the method provides a practical way to retain complementary alignment signals while keeping the selected training set compact. We see no significant risk of direct negative societal im-pact when using it to improve the efficiency of ex-isting alignment workflows from this data selec-tion method.

A.1 Data Selection for Instruction Tuning and Alignment 

Existing methods can be broadly categorized based on the type of signal used to guide selection. 

Distribution- and representation-based methods. A line of work models datasets as distributions in embedding space and selects sam-ples to maximize diversity and coverage. Methods such as diversity metrics (Yang et al., 2025b), stratified sampling (Mirza et al., 2025), and QDIT (Bukharin et al., 2024) characterize global data distributions, while sparse autoencoder-based approaches (Yang et al., 2025a) learn latent fac-tors and prioritize underrepresented dimensions. These methods effectively improve coverage in representation space, but operate at a coarse level and do not explicitly capture alignment-relevant preference structure. 

Training-signal-based methods. Another line of work leverages training dynamics, such as gra-dients or loss signals, to identify informative sam-ples. TAGCOS (Zhang et al., 2025) clusters sam-ples based on gradient embeddings to preserve di-versity in parameter update directions, while prin-cipled data selection (Gao et al., 2025) analyzes sample difficulty to filter unstable examples. How-ever, these methods typically require additional training or repeated model updates to obtain reli-able signals, making them computationally expen-sive and tightly coupled with the training process. 

Scalar scoring and heuristic filtering meth-ods. Some approaches rely on scalar scoring func-tions to rank and select samples. DEITA (Liu et al., 2024a) combines quality, complexity, and diversity signals, often relying on external teacher models or LLM-based judges, while Less-Is-More (Liu et al., 2024b) ranks samples using model-internal value signals such as difficulty or influence. These methods are simple and efficient, but treat samples independently and ignore struc-tural redundancy, making them prone to biased se-lection and dataset collapse. 

Summary. Overall, existing methods rely on generic distributional, training, or scalar signals, without modeling the intrinsic structure of pref-erence data. In particular, they do not account for multi-dataset settings where preference sig-nals consist of both a dominant anchor geometry and dataset-specific residual variation. In contrast, our method explicitly models this structure by de-composing representations into anchor and resid-ual components, enabling principled data selec-tion that preserves global alignment consistency while maintaining heterogeneous coverage across datasets. 

A.2 Our Position 

In contrast to prior work, we explicitly model safety preference data as a structured geometric object in representation space. Our method decom-poses preference signals into anchor and residual components. The base variant DOG operates only on simple scalar anchor/residual scores, while 

DOG-D further introduces our custom direction-wise feature representation and a diversity regular-ization term, enabling both structural coverage and redundancy reduction in a unified framework.
