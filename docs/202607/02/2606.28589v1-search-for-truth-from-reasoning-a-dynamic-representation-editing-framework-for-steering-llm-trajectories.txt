# Abstract 

Current approaches to enhance Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning, such as Chain-of-Thought and “Wait” prompts, primarily encour-age models to think more , yet often fail to guide them toward Truth . While Representation Editing (RepE) offers a intrinsic control, its application to dynamic reasoning trajectories remains underex-plored. In this work, we bridge this gap by investi-gating the geometry of truth within unfolding rea-soning chains. We uncover three critical insights: (1) Truth is encoded at the sentence level and is entangled with latent reasoning patterns; (2) Ef-fective intervention follows an Uncertainty Prin-ciple and a Decay Effect , requiring localization to early, high-entropy forks; (3) Naive steering vec-tors suffer from noise, risking collateral damage to correct trajectories. Based on these findings, we propose DynaSteer , a dynamic RepE frame-work. DynaSteer employs pattern clustering to disentangle reasoning manifolds and utilizes Fisher-LDA to project purified truth. By dynami-cally monitoring lookahead entropy, it selectively steers and rolls back trajectories only when neces-sary. Comprehensive experimental results on sev-eral MATH benchmark verify the effectiveness of 

DynaSteer , and experiments on out-of-domain coding tasks further confirm its generalization ability. Our code is publicly available at https: //github.com/tianlwang/DynaSteer .

# 1. Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated re-markable proficiency in complex reasoning tasks (Huang & Chang, 2023; Plaat et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025b), largely driven by the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) paradigm (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2025b; Fang et al., 2026a) which elicits structured reasoning trajectories. Recent ad-vancements (Yang et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025; Cai et al., 2015; Pipis et al., 2025) have further attempted to enhance this capability through inspired prompting strategies, such as the “Aha moment” or “Wait” prompts. While these meth-ods successfully encourage LLMs to generate more tokens and engage in deeper contemplation, they primarily induce the model to think more without explicitly guiding it to steer toward correct deductions among bifurcating possibil-ities. Consequently, when LLMs face uncertainty, simply extending the generation length often leads to “hallucination snowballs” (Zhong et al., 2024) rather than self-correction. To address this limitation, Representation Editing (RepE) (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025c; Ma et al., 2025) has emerged as a promising avenue for intrinsic control. Unlike prompting, RepE directly pinpoints and modulates the internal activation patterns of LLMs, offering a more fundamental remedy by guiding the hidden states toward a target concept. However, a critical gap remains in the litera-1

Prior works (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025c) predomi-nantly focus on non-reasoning modes or contexts where the conclusion is explicitly visible. The challenge of steering the model towards Truth during the intermediate deriva-tion stage, before a final answer is reached, presents unique spatiotemporal complexities.

In this work, we conduct a systematic exploration of ap-plying RepE to guide LLM reasoning. Our investigation is structured around three pivotal research questions, Exis-tence , Locus , and Mitigation , that bridge the gap between static attribute editing and dynamic reasoning guidance: 1. Existence of Truth : Does the Truth exist within LLM representations along reasoning trajectories? A fun-damental prerequisite for RepE is that internal repre-sentations must distinguish between Truth and Fallacy .Our probing experiments reveal a crucial nuance: naive token-level probes struggle to discern Truth , suggesting it is not encoded in atomic units. However, when ele-vated to the sentence level, the latent distinction becomes evident. We further discover that the Truth Steering is in-herently entangled with specific reasoning patterns. By disentangling these patterns via clustering, we observe a significant enhancement in discrimination accuracy. 2. Locus of Intervention : Where and when is interven-tion effective? We identify two critical spatiotemporal dynamics that govern effective steering. First, the Decay Effect : intervention is most potent in the early stages of reasoning; as the chain lengthens, the model becomes “entrenched” in its trajectory, rendering late-stage editing ineffective. Second, the Uncertainty Principle : effective intervention strictly correlates with high entropy. Editing is necessary only at “reasoning forks” where the model exhibits uncertainty; intervening at low-entropy positions yields negligible benefits or disrupts coherence. 3. Mitigation of Risk : How to mitigate the potential risks associated with intervention? Standard RepE methods typically derive steering vectors using a naive Mean-Difference approach. However, given the entanglement of Truth with other high-level semantic concepts, this approach inevitably incorporates noise, posing a risk of corrupting originally correct trajectories. To mitigate this, we propose the use of Fisher Linear Discriminant Analysis (Fisher-LDA) . By projecting representations onto a purified subspace, we maximize the separation of Truth while minimizing interference with the model’s semantic manifold. Based on these insights, we propose DynaSteer .

DynaSteer is a dynamic inference-time RepE framework designed to steer reasoning trajectories proactively. It oper-ates by (1) detecting critical reasoning forks via sentence-level lookahead entropy; (2) calculating purified Truth Steer-ing using Fisher-LDA within disentangled reasoning clus-ters; and (3) applying a temporally decaying injection to guide the model’s trajectory while employing a rollback mechanism to correct errors at their inception. In summary, our main contributions are as follows: • Systematic Analysis of Dynamic RepE: We provide the first comprehensive analysis bridging static attribute edit-ing and dynamic reasoning. We reveal the Decay Effect 

and the Uncertainty Principle , establishing that interven-tion must be localized to early, high-entropy reasoning forks to be effective. • Subspace-Projected Steering Mechanism: We identify that the geometric entanglement of truth and reasoning patterns limits standard RepE. We propose a novel appli-cation of Fisher-LDA to project internal representations onto a discriminative subspace, ensuring safe and precise steering. • DynaSteer Framework: Synthesizing these findings, we introduce DynaSteer , an adaptive framework that dynamically monitors uncertainty and intervenes only when necessary. Extensive evaluations conducted in both in-domain and out-of-domain scenarios show that our approach substantially outperforms existing baselines on complex reasoning benchmarks.

We term this phenomenon the Decay Effect . It suggests that autoregressive reasoning exhibits strong path dependence. As the model generates more tokens consistent with an ini-tial fallacy, its internal state becomes “entrenched” in that error. The hidden states ht at later time steps t ≫ 1 are con-ditioned on a long history of incorrect tokens x<t , making the incorrect trajectory a deep local minimum in the energy landscape. This implies that the “window of opportunity” for effective steering is transient. Interventions must be an-ticipatory : corrections are most feasible at the inception of a reasoning error ( T1), before the trajectory collapses into a deep local minimum from which even stochastic exploration cannot escape. 

Summary. The integration of spatial and temporal analyses defines a precise regime for efficient RepE. 

Takeaway : Effective intervention is highly localized in both space and time. Spatially, editing is strictly necessary only at high-entropy reasoning forks. Temporally, inter-vention must occur early in the derivation process, as the model’s susceptibility to correction decays rapidly due to the entrenchment of logical errors over time.

signal (Sec. 3) and the spatiotemporal constraints of inter-vention (Sec. 4), we introduce DynaSteer , a framework that bridges the gap between static representation editing and dynamic reasoning trajectories.

6.2. Truth -Relevant Head Location 

Not all attention heads encode the Truth signal equally. To ensure computational efficiency and intervention precision,

6.4. Dynamic Reasoning via Aggregated Steering 

During the online inference phase, DynaSteer applies the pre-computed steering vectors dynamically. This process is governed by entropy monitoring and a rollback mechanism to handle the path-dependent nature of generation. 

Lookahead Entropy Monitoring. To avoid unnecessary perturbations, DynaSteer adopts a selective intervention strategy. As the model generates a candidate sentence Scand ,the framework computes its average token entropy H(Scand ).It employs a threshold γ, set to the 80 th percentile of the training entropy distribution, following prior work (Wang et al., 2025a). • If H(Scand ) ≤ γ, the sentence is deemed deterministic and reliable; generation proceeds normally. • If H(Scand ) > γ, the sentence represents a high-uncertainty reasoning fork, triggering the intervention mechanism.

# 8. Limitation, Discussion and Conclusion 

Despite the promising results obtained in our work, it is im-portant to acknowledge the limitations. Current mainstream research regarding guiding LLMs through reasoning forks predominately relies on Reinforcement Learning (RL). No-tably, Wang et al. (2025a) explicitly demonstrates that lever-aging GRPO on reasoning forking tokens can substantially 8Search for Truth from Reasoning: A Dynamic Representation Editing Framework for Steering LLM Trajectories 

elevate the model’s reasoning capabilities. We compared 

DynaSteer against GRPO under identical experimental settings. The results indicate that GRPO achieves a perfor-mance of 84 .6% (6.4% ↑), whereas DynaSteer achieves 

82 .3% (4.0% ↑). We hypothesize that this performance gap primarily stems from the geometric nature of the Truth .

DynaSteer assumes that Truth Steering can be captured by linear steering vectors . However, the actual manifold of 

Truth within complex reasoning trajectories involves high-dimensional, non-linear dependencies . RL methods like GRPO update the underlying policy parameters, enabling the model to internalize these complex, non-linear mappings more effectively than an external linear intervention. Nevertheless, we claim a stark contrast on computational cost: GRPO requires approximately 160GB of VRAM and spans up to 15 hours of training time to converge. In con-trast, DynaSteer is a training-free framework that directly modulates the LLM during inference. Consequently, while RL methods offer a higher upper bound in performance, 

DynaSteer makes a distinct contribution to resource-constrained communities. 

Conclusion. In this work, we present a systematic explo-ration of Representation Edit into the dynamics of Truth 

within LLM reasoning trajectories, moving beyond static attribute editing to dynamic process control. Our investi-gation establishes the theoretical foundations for dynamic intervention: confirming that the Truth exists but is geomet-rically entangled with reasoning patterns, and revealing that the window for effective correction is transient, constrained by the Decay Effect .To operationalize these insights, we introduce DynaSteer ,a RepE framework that leverages pattern clustering and Fisher-LDA, DynaSteer successfully disentangles the 

Truth Steering from other high-level semantic concepts, ad-dressing the safety risks inherent in naive representation editing. Furthermore, our entropy-aware monitoring en-sures that intervention is precise and economical, applied only at critical reasoning forks. Empirical results across diverse benchmarks confirm that DynaSteer not only mit-igates hallucinations but also achieves superior performance compared to strong baselines. Ultimately, this work of-fers a resource-efficient alternative to reinforcement learn-ing for steering LLMs, paving the way for more reliable, self-correcting AI systems that seek Truth from the very inception of their reasoning.

# A. Related Work 

Representation Editing for Truth Representation Editing (RepE) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for interpretability and control, aiming to locate and intervene on high-level concepts encoded within the linear activation space of LLMs (Zou et al., 2023; Liao et al., 2026a; Fang et al., 2026b). In the context of Truth , Li et al. (2023) proposed Inference-Time Intervention (ITI), which identifies attention heads correlated with truthfulness and shifts their activations to suppress hallucinations. Similarly, Marks & Tegmark (2023) investigated the geometry of truth, revealing that truthful statements often align along specific linear directions in the representation space regardless of the topic. However, a critical limitation of these approaches is their reliance on static editing derived from datasets containing explicit answers or simple true/false statements. There is a notable lack of exploration into dynamic representation editing, specifically regarding how to calculate truth directions within unfolding reasoning trajectories where the final answer is latent and not yet observable. 

Reinforcing LLM Reasoning Enhancing the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models has predominantly relied on Reinforcement Learning (RL) paradigms. Recent advancements have shifted focus from outcome-based supervision to process-oriented approaches, where the model is guided at critical “reasoning forks”. For instance, Lightman et al. (2023) demonstrated that Process Reward Models (PRMs) can significantly improve mathematical reasoning by verifying intermediate steps. Furthermore, Shao et al. (2024) introduced Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which leverages group-level comparisons at reasoning forks to efficiently steer the model toward correct trajectories, yielding substantial performance gains. Wang et al. (2025a) further demonstrates that leveraging GRPO on reasoning forking tokens can substantially elevate the model’s reasoning capabilities. Despite their effectiveness, these RL-based methods suffer from prohibitive computational costs, often requiring massive GPU resources (e.g., hundreds of gigabytes of VRAM) and extensive training time, rendering them largely inaccessible to resource-constrained research communities.

C.1. Algorithm Description 

The DynaSteer framework operates in two distinct phases: an offline preparation phase for steering vector derivation and an online inference phase for dynamic intervention. The complete procedure is outlined in Algorithm 1. In the Offline Phase , we first construct a consistency-based dataset by filtering reasoning trajectories that lead to consensus answers. We then identify the truth-relevant heads ( Htop ) via linear probing. To handle reasoning heterogeneity, difference vectors are clustered into M patterns. For each cluster, we compute a purified steering vector vm using Fisher-LDA to maximize the separation between Truth and Fallacy while minimizing within-class variance. In the Online Phase , the model generates reasoning chains sentence by sentence. We monitor the entropy H(Scand ) of each candidate sentence. If the entropy exceeds the threshold γ (indicating a reasoning fork) and the step is within the effective temporal window ( β(t) = 1 ), we trigger the intervention. The current hidden state is projected onto the pre-computed clusters to determine adaptive strengths ηm, and the aggregated steering is applied. Crucially, a rollback mechanism is employed: the unsteered candidate sentence is discarded, and the model regenerates the step using the edited representation 

˜h.

# E. Experimental Setups 

Network Architecture, Datasets and Metrics Our experiments were based on various backbone LLMs, including Qwen3 series (Bai et al., 2023) of various sizes (Qwen3-1.7B and Qwen3-8B) and LLaMA3.2-3B-Instruct (Touvron et al., 2023). We collected Truth and Fallacy representation using GSM8K (Li et al., 2024) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) training set to calculate Truth Steering . We introduced two types of tasks, Math (in-distribution) and Coding (out-of-distribution), to assess the effectiveness of DynaSteer . For the Math tasks, we considered the following datasets: GSM8K test part, MATH500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and AMC23 (Hendrycks et al., 2021). For the Coding tasks, we considered the HumanEval+ (Chen et al., 2021) and MBPP+ (Austin et al., 2021) datasets. We evaluated these datasets with pass@1 accuracy. 

Baselines We adopt the following state-of-the-art approaches as our compared baselines. • Plain : Plain does not use any techniques, LLM directly reason and answer questions. • “Wait” : “Wait” monitors entropy in real-time and manually injects Wait prompts at high entropy locations to guide LLM to perform more thinking. • RepE literature : We adopt several recently proposed RepE literature as our baseline models, including ITI (Li et al., 2023), ACT (Wang et al., 2025c) and DRESS (Ma et al., 2025). The details of the baseline RepE algorithm we use is as follows: 1. ITI (Li et al., 2023) (NeurIPS’23): Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) is a lightweight method that improves the truthfulness of large language models by adjusting internal activations along learned directions at inference time, using only a small subset of attention heads. ITI also exposes a controllable trade-off between truthfulness and helpfulness by tuning the intervention strength, suggesting that LLMs encode latent representations of truth internally. 2. ACT (Wang et al., 2025c) (WWW’25): Adaptive Activation Steering (ACT) is a tuning-free inference-time method that enhances LLM truthfulness by adaptively steering model activations toward linearly encoded Truth Steering . By leveraging multiple truthfulness-related steering vectors and dynamically adjusting intervention strength, ACT effectively mitigates diverse hallucination types. As a plug-and-play add-on, it achieves substantial truthfulness gains across a wide range of models and scales robustly to large LLMs. 3. DRESS (Ma et al., 2025) (ICLR’25): DRESS is a lightweight, train-free representation editing method for generating stylized LLM responses while preserving the original semantics. It disentangles a style-relevant subspace in the model’s representations and applies adaptively scaled steering vectors to balance stylistic fidelity and semantic integrity. 

Implement Details We implemented our framework using PyTorch and the Hugging Face transformers library (Wolf, 2019). To enable high-throughput inference, we integrated vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) as the generation backend. For mathe-matical reasoning benchmarks, we utilized vLLM for sampling and employed the math-verify library 1 to rigorously parse and validate answer correctness. For code generation tasks, we strictly followed the evaluation protocols and settings established by EvalPlus (Liu et al., 2023). All experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA A800 (80GB) GPU. 

Construction of Contrastive Pairs. To reliably estimate the Truth Steering vector, we meticulously construct positive and negative contrastive samples from the training set. Specifically, we prompt the LLM to generate reasoning trajectories. At each generated sentence boundary, we compute the lookahead entropy. When the entropy exceeds a pre-defined threshold— indicating a critical reasoning fork—we pause the primary generation and conduct N independent stochastic rollouts from that exact state (in our experiments, N = 10 ). We then evaluate the final answers of all N rollouts. If all rollouts yield the correct answer, the hidden representations of the current reasoning fork are assigned the label Truth (+1 ). Conversely, if all rollouts yield an incorrect answer, they are labeled as Fallacy (−1). To ensure geometric purity of the representations and to eliminate noise, any ambiguous cases—where the rollouts produce a mix of correct and incorrect answers—are strictly discarded. This highly rigorous filtering strategy guarantees that the resulting contrastive pair dataset robustly captures the intrinsic directional difference between truth and fallacy.
